# Pattern: Simple Indirect Proportion

## 1. Pattern Overview
- This pattern solves problems where two quantities are linked such that an increase in one causes a proportional decrease in the other, and vice-versa.
- Understanding this pattern is essential for mastering efficiency, resource allocation, and time-management calculations in competitive mathematics.

## 2. Key Formulas
For two quantities $A$ and $B$ that are indirectly proportional:

- **The Product Constant Rule:**
  $$A_1 \times B_1 = A_2 \times B_2$$
  *(Where $A_1, B_1$ are initial values and $A_2, B_2$ are final values)*

- **The Ratio Method:**
  $$\frac{A_1}{A_2} = \frac{B_2}{B_1}$$
  *(Note the inversion: the ratio of the first quantity is equal to the inverse ratio of the second)*

## 3. When to Use This Pattern
- **Keywords:** "Same work," "Same distance," "Finish a job," "Required to do the same."
- **Recognition Clues:** Look for scenarios involving workers and time, speed and travel time, or fixed resources being distributed.
- **Given vs. Found:** You are typically given an initial pair of values $(A_1, B_1)$ and one value of the second state $(A_2)$, and you must find the missing value $(B_2)$.

## 4. Core Concept & Theory
The fundamental idea is **Inverse Variation**. In direct proportion, the ratio remains constant ($\frac{A}{B} = k$). In indirect proportion, the **product** remains constant ($A \times B = k$). This works because if you have more workers, the time required to complete a fixed task must decrease to keep the total "work units" constant.

## 5. Step-by-Step Solution Method
1. **Identify the variables:** Assign $A$ and $B$ to the two quantities (e.g., Men and Days).
2. **Determine the relationship:** Ask, "If I increase $A$, does $B$ decrease?" If yes, it is Indirect Proportion.
3. **Set up the equation:** Use the product rule $A_1 \times B_1 = A_2 \times B_2$.
4. **Substitute values:** Plug in the known numbers.
5. **Solve for $x$:** Isolate the unknown variable using basic algebra.

## 6. Worked Examples

**Example 1: Workforce Calculation**
*If 36 men can do a piece of work in 25 hours, in how many hours will 15 men do it?*
- **Given:** $M_1 = 36, T_1 = 25$; $M_2 = 15, T_2 = x$
- **Reasoning:** Fewer men will take more time (Indirect).
- **Solution:** 
  $36 \times 25 = 15 \times x$
  $900 = 15x$
  $x = \frac{900}{15} = 60$
- **Result:** 15 men will take 60 hours.

**Example 2: Resource Allocation**
*35 women can finish a task in 15 days. How many women are needed to finish it in 25 days?*
- **Given:** $W_1 = 35, D_1 = 15$; $W_2 = x, D_2 = 25$
- **Reasoning:** More days allowed means fewer women are required (Indirect).
- **Solution:**
  $35 \times 15 = x \times 25$
  $525 = 25x$
  $x = \frac{525}{25} = 21$
- **Result:** 21 women are required.

## 7. Recognition Clues & Keywords
- **Signals:** "Same work," "Same distance," "Inversely proportional."
- **Distinction:** Do not confuse with **Direct Proportion**, where more of one leads to more of the other (e.g., more items = more cost). If the total "output" is fixed (like a single job), it is almost always Indirect.

## 8. Common Mistakes
1. **Directly multiplying ratios:** Students often write $\frac{A_1}{A_2} = \frac{B_1}{B_2}$. This is for Direct Proportion. Always invert one side for Indirect.
2. **Ignoring the "Fixed" constraint:** Assuming a problem is indirect when the work is not fixed. Always check if the total work changes.
3. **Calculation errors:** Forgetting to divide by the coefficient of $x$ after multiplying the initial pair.

## 9. Practice Tips
- **The "Logic Check":** After finding your answer, ask: "Does this make sense?" If you have fewer workers, your time answer *must* be higher than the original time. If it isn't, you likely used the wrong proportion.
- **Related Patterns:** Study **Direct Proportion** and **Chain Rule** (for problems with more than two variables) to build a complete toolkit.
