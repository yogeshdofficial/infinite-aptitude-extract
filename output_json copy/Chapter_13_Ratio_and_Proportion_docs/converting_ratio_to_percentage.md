# Pattern: Converting Ratio to Percentage

## 1. Pattern Overview
This pattern provides a systematic method to transform a ratio of two quantities into a percentage representation, allowing for easier comparison and interpretation of parts relative to a whole. It is essential for financial analysis, data interpretation, and probability calculations where parts of a total need to be expressed as a standard percentage.

## 2. Key Formulas
To convert a ratio $a : b$ into a percentage, we treat the ratio as a fraction and multiply by 100.

**Formula for the first term as a percentage of the total:**
$$\text{Percentage of } a = \left( \frac{a}{a + b} \right) \times 100$$

**Formula for the second term as a percentage of the total:**
$$\text{Percentage of } b = \left( \frac{b}{a + b} \right) \times 100$$

---

## 3. When to Use This Pattern
- **Keywords:** "What percentage of the total...", "Express the ratio as a percentage", "Share of the total".
- **Recognition:** Use this when you are given a ratio (e.g., $3:2$) and asked to find what portion of the whole each part represents.
- **Given:** The ratio $a : b$.
- **To Find:** The percentage contribution of $a$ or $b$ relative to the sum $(a+b)$.

---

## 4. Core Concept & Theory
A ratio $a : b$ represents two parts of a whole. The "whole" is the sum of the parts, which is $a + b$. To find the percentage, we calculate the fraction of the specific part over the total sum and scale it to 100. This works because a percentage is simply a fraction with a denominator of 100.

---

## 5. Step-by-Step Solution Method
1. **Identify the parts:** Extract the values $a$ and $b$ from the given ratio $a : b$.
2. **Calculate the total:** Add the terms together to find the whole: $\text{Total} = a + b$.
3. **Form the fraction:** Place the part you are interested in over the total: $\frac{\text{Part}}{\text{Total}}$.
4. **Convert to percentage:** Multiply the resulting fraction by 100.
5. **Verification:** Ensure the sum of the calculated percentages equals 100%.

---

## 6. Worked Examples

**Example 1: Converting a simple ratio**
Given: A mixture contains milk and water in the ratio $3 : 2$. Find the percentage of milk in the mixture.
Solution:
1. Parts: $a = 3$ (milk), $b = 2$ (water).
2. Total: $3 + 2 = 5$.
3. Fraction of milk: $\frac{3}{5}$.
4. Percentage: $\frac{3}{5} \times 100 = 60\%$.

**Example 2: Calculating both parts**
Given: Two candidates A and B received votes in the ratio $7 : 3$. What percentage of the total votes did each receive?
Solution:
1. Total parts: $7 + 3 = 10$.
2. Percentage of A: $\frac{7}{10} \times 100 = 70\%$.
3. Percentage of B: $\frac{3}{10} \times 100 = 30\%$.
4. Check: $70\% + 30\% = 100\%$.

---

## 7. Recognition Clues & Keywords
- **Look for:** "Ratio of X to Y", "Distributed in the ratio".
- **Distinction:** Do not confuse this with "Percentage Increase/Decrease." This pattern is strictly for **part-to-whole** conversion. If the problem asks for the percentage *change*, use the formula $\frac{\text{Difference}}{\text{Original}} \times 100$.

---

## 8. Common Mistakes
1. **Using only one term as the denominator:** Students often use $\frac{a}{b} \times 100$ instead of $\frac{a}{a+b} \times 100$. Remember: the denominator must be the **total** of all parts.
2. **Forgetting to sum the ratio:** Always add the terms of the ratio first to define the "whole."
3. **Calculation errors:** Ensure the multiplication by 100 is applied to the entire fraction.
4. **Misinterpreting the question:** Ensure you are calculating the percentage of the *part* requested, not the other part.

---

## 9. Practice Tips
- **Mastery:** Practice converting ratios with more than two terms (e.g., $1:2:3$). The logic remains the same: $\frac{\text{Part}}{\text{Sum of all parts}} \times 100$.
- **Related Patterns:** Study **Proportion** (Section II) and **Comparison of Ratios** (Section IV) to understand how these values behave when scaled.
- **Mental Math:** Memorize common fraction-to-percentage conversions (e.g., $1/4 = 25\%$, $1/5 = 20\%$) to speed up your calculations.
