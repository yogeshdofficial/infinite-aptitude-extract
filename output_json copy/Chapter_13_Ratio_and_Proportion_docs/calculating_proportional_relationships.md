# Pattern Name: Calculating Proportional Relationships

## 1. Pattern Overview
This pattern provides a systematic framework for solving problems involving the comparison of quantities, the equality of ratios, and the behavior of variables under direct or inverse variation. Mastering this pattern is essential for solving complex quantitative problems involving mixtures, scaling, and physical relationships.

## 2. Key Formulas

*   **Proportion Equality:** If $a:b :: c:d$, then:
    $$bc = ad$$
*   **Fourth Proportional ($d$):** Given $a, b, c$:
    $$d = \frac{bc}{a}$$
*   **Third Proportional ($c$):** Given $a, b$:
    $$c = \frac{b^2}{a}$$
*   **Mean Proportional:** Between $a$ and $b$:
    $$x = \sqrt{ab}$$
*   **Compounded Ratio:** Of $(a:b), (c:d), (e:f)$:
    $$\text{Ratio} = \frac{ace}{bdf}$$
*   **Variation:**
    *   Direct: $x = ky$
    *   Inverse: $xy = k$
*   **Mixture Replacement:** After $n$ operations of replacing $y$ units from $x$ total:
    $$\text{Pure Liquid} = x \left(1 - \frac{y}{x}\right)^n$$

## 3. When to Use This Pattern
*   **Keywords:** "Proportional to," "Ratio of," "Mean proportional," "Compounded," "Replaced by water," "Directly/Inversely varies."
*   **Recognition:** Use when a problem describes a relationship between two or more quantities that change in tandem or when you need to find a missing term in a sequence of ratios.
*   **Given vs. Found:** Typically, you are given initial values or a constant of variation and asked to find a missing term ($a, b, c,$ or $d$) or the final concentration of a mixture.

## 4. Core Concept & Theory
The core of this pattern is the **invariance of ratios**. Whether quantities are scaled up or down, the relationship between them remains constant. By treating ratios as fractions, we can use algebraic manipulation to isolate unknown variables. The "Product of Means = Product of Extremes" rule is the fundamental tool for solving any proportion problem.

## 5. Step-by-Step Solution Method
1.  **Identify the Relationship:** Determine if the problem involves a simple ratio, a proportion, or a variation (direct/inverse).
2.  **Set up the Equation:** Assign variables to the knowns and the unknown. If it is a proportion, write it as $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$.
3.  **Apply the Rule:** 
    *   If finding a missing term, use $bc = ad$.
    *   If dealing with variation, solve for the constant $k$ first.
    *   If dealing with mixtures, use the power formula $n$.
4.  **Simplify:** Perform the arithmetic to isolate the unknown variable.
5.  **Verify Units:** Ensure all quantities are in the same units before calculating.

## 6. Worked Examples

**Example 1: Finding the Fourth Proportional**
*Given:* Find the fourth proportional to 4, 9, and 12.
*Solution:*
1. Let the fourth proportional be $x$.
2. Set up the proportion: $4:9 :: 12:x$.
3. Apply the rule: $4 \times x = 9 \times 12$.
4. Solve: $4x = 108 \implies x = 27$.

**Example 2: Mixture Replacement**
*Given:* A container has 50L of milk. 10L is removed and replaced with water. This is done twice. Find the final milk quantity.
*Solution:*
1. Use the formula: $\text{Final} = x(1 - \frac{y}{x})^n$.
2. Substitute: $50(1 - \frac{10}{50})^2$.
3. Simplify: $50(1 - 0.2)^2 = 50(0.8)^2 = 50(0.64)$.
4. Result: $32\text{L}$.

## 7. Recognition Clues & Keywords
*   **"Mean proportional":** Always implies $\sqrt{ab}$.
*   **"Compounded ratio":** Always implies multiplying the fractions.
*   **"Directly proportional":** As one increases, the other increases (division is constant).
*   **"Inversely proportional":** As one increases, the other decreases (multiplication is constant).
*   **Avoid:** Do not confuse "Duplicate ratio" ($a^2:b^2$) with "Sub-duplicate ratio" ($\sqrt{a}:\sqrt{b}$).

## 8. Common Mistakes
*   **Unit Mismatch:** Trying to calculate a ratio when units are different (e.g., meters vs. centimeters). Always convert to the same unit first.
*   **Inverse vs. Direct:** Assuming variables are directly proportional when they are inversely proportional. Check: If $x$ goes up and $y$ goes down, it is inverse.
*   **Order of Terms:** In $a:b :: c:d$, students often swap the means and extremes. Remember: $a$ and $d$ are the "outer" (extremes), $b$ and $c$ are the "inner" (means).
*   **Mixture Formula:** Forgetting to raise the fraction to the power of $n$ (number of operations).

## 9. Practice Tips
*   **Master the Fractions:** Treat every ratio as a fraction; it makes cross-multiplication intuitive.
*   **Check for $k$:** In variation problems, always find the constant $k$ first; it is the "key" to the entire problem.
*   **Related Patterns:** Study "Percentage Change" and "Time and Work," as they rely heavily on proportional reasoning.
