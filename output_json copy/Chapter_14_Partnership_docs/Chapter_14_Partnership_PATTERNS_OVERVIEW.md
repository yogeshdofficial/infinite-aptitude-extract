# Chapter 14: Partnership

## 1. Chapter Introduction
This chapter explores the mathematical principles behind business partnerships. A partnership occurs when two or more individuals join forces to run a business. Understanding how to fairly distribute profits or losses based on individual contributions—whether in the form of capital or time—is essential for financial literacy and solving competitive examination problems.

## 2. Core Ideas
The fundamental principle of a partnership is that **profit is shared in proportion to the contribution of each partner.** 
*   **Simple Partnership:** All partners invest their capital for the same duration. Profit is divided strictly based on the ratio of their investments.
*   **Compound Partnership:** Partners invest different amounts for different lengths of time. Here, we must calculate "equivalent capital" by multiplying the investment amount by the time period.
*   **Roles:** Partners may be "working partners" (who manage the business and may receive extra compensation) or "sleeping partners" (who only provide capital).

## 3. Important Formulas
To calculate the share of profit, use the following logic:

**For Simple Partnership:**
$$\text{Ratio of Profit} = \text{Ratio of Investments}$$
*Use this when all partners keep their money in the business for the same amount of time.*

**For Compound Partnership:**
$$\text{Ratio of Profit} = (\text{Capital}_1 \times \text{Time}_1) : (\text{Capital}_2 \times \text{Time}_2) : \dots$$
*Use this when partners join at different times or withdraw/add capital during the year.*

## 4. Important Facts and Shortcuts
*   **Equivalent Capital:** Always convert investments into a common unit of time (e.g., months) before calculating ratios.
*   **Management Fees:** If a partner is a "working partner" and receives a percentage of the profit for management, subtract this amount from the total profit *before* distributing the balance among partners based on their capital ratios.
*   **Capital Adjustments:** If a partner changes their investment amount mid-year, calculate the total "capital-months" by summing the products of each investment amount and the time it was held.

## 5. How to Recognize the Problem Type
*   **"Started a business together":** Look for simple ratio distribution.
*   **"Joined after X months":** This is a Compound Partnership; you must adjust the time duration for each partner.
*   **"Withdrew/Invested more":** This indicates a change in capital; calculate the weighted average (capital × time) for each period.
*   **"Working partner gets X%":** Look for problems involving management commissions or salaries before profit splitting.

## 6. Common Mistakes
*   **Ignoring Time:** Assuming profit is shared only by the initial investment amount, forgetting that time is a critical factor in compound partnerships.
*   **Miscalculating Time:** Failing to correctly count the number of months a partner’s money was actually in the business (e.g., if someone joins after 3 months, their money is only invested for 9 months).
*   **Forgetting the Balance:** In problems with management commissions, students often distribute the *total* profit by capital ratio instead of the *remaining* profit after the working partner's share is deducted.
*   **Ratio Errors:** Simplifying ratios incorrectly or failing to convert fractional capital ratios into whole numbers before proceeding.

## 7. Chapter Pattern Map
This chapter covers a wide range of scenarios, including:
*   **Basic Profit Distribution:** Standard capital-based splits.
*   **Dynamic Adjustments:** Handling mid-year capital additions or withdrawals.
*   **Variable Solving:** Finding missing time or capital values.
*   **Management Incentives:** Handling salaries or commissions for working partners.
*   **Complex Scenarios:** Rent sharing and equivalent unit calculations.
