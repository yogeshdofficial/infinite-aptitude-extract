# Profit Rate Capital

## Overview

*   This pattern involves business partners where profit distribution is fixed by a ratio, and a change in the "rate of profit" (percentage return) causes a specific change in an individual's income.
*   The central idea is that an individual's profit share is a fixed fraction of the total profit, where total profit is defined as a percentage of the total invested capital.

---

## Recognition Clues

*   **Keywords:** "Rate of profit rises from X% to Y%", "Income of [Partner] will increase by [Value]".
*   **Given:** A fixed fractional share of total profit for each partner and the absolute increase in one partner's income due to a percentage rate change.
*   **Asked:** The total capital invested or the capital of a specific partner.

---

## Important Formulas

### [Individual Profit Share]

$$
\text{Individual Profit} = (\text{Fractional Share}) \times (\text{Rate} \times \text{Total Capital})
$$

*Meaning:* The profit received by a partner is their specific share of the total business return.
*Use when:* Calculating the impact of a change in the profit rate on a specific partner's income.

---

## Solution Framework

1.  Determine the fractional profit share for the partner mentioned in the problem.
2.  Calculate the difference in the profit rate (e.g., $Y\% - X\%$).
3.  Set up the equation: $(\text{Fractional Share}) \times (\text{Rate Difference} \%) \times (\text{Total Capital}) = \text{Income Increase}$.
4.  Solve for the Total Capital ($x$).
5.  Multiply the Total Capital by the specific partner's fractional share to find their individual capital.

---

## Shortcut Tricks

*   **The "Total Capital" Shortcut:** Instead of calculating individual capitals first, find the Total Capital ($x$) directly using the rate difference and the partner's share.
*   **Why:** It avoids redundant calculations by isolating the variable $x$ in a single linear equation.

---

## Common Mistakes

*   **Mistake:** Applying the rate change to the individual's capital instead of the total capital.
    *   *Reason:* Misinterpreting "rate of profit" as an interest rate on individual investment rather than a return on total business capital.
    *   *Fix:* Always apply the percentage rate to the total capital ($x$) first, then multiply by the partner's share.
*   **Mistake:** Forgetting to calculate the "remainder" share correctly.
    *   *Reason:* Rushing the subtraction of the primary partner's share from the whole ($1$).
    *   *Fix:* Explicitly write $1 - (\text{Gordon's share})$ before dividing the remainder.

---

## Similar Patterns

*   This is distinct from standard Partnership problems (where time and investment ratios are given) because it introduces a **variable percentage rate** applied to the total capital.

---

## Revision Summary

**Key formula:** $\text{Share} \times (\Delta \text{Rate} \%) \times \text{Total Capital} = \Delta \text{Income}$.

**Spot it by:** A change in profit percentage causing a specific dollar/rupee increase in income.

**Fastest method:** Solve for Total Capital ($x$) first, then distribute it by the profit ratio.

**Biggest trap:** Applying the percentage rate change to an individual's capital instead of the total capital.
