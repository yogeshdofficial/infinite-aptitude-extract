# Relative Investment

## Overview

These questions involve partners whose investments are defined by relationships to each other (e.g., "A is 3 times B") rather than absolute values. The central idea is to express all individual investments in terms of a single variable ($x$) to establish a fixed ratio.

---

## Recognition Clues

* **Keywords:** "3 times as much as," "two-thirds of," "x less than," "twice A's equals thrice B's."
* **Given:** A set of comparative constraints between partners' capitals or time periods.
* **Asked:** The share of a specific partner given the total profit.
* **Structure:** Often involves a chain of dependencies (e.g., A depends on B, B depends on C).

---

## Important Formulas

### Profit Distribution Ratio
$$
\text{Share of Partner} = \text{Total Profit} \times \left( \frac{\text{Individual Ratio}}{\text{Sum of Ratios}} \right)
$$
*Meaning:* The profit is divided proportionally based on the ratio of (Capital $\times$ Time).
*Use when:* You have calculated the final ratio of investments for all partners.

---

## Solution Framework

1. Assign a variable ($x$) to the partner with the "base" or "smallest" share.
2. Express all other partners' investments as multiples or fractions of $x$.
3. Simplify the resulting expressions into a clean integer ratio ($A:B:C$).
4. Sum the ratio parts to find the denominator.
5. Multiply the total profit by the specific partner's fraction.

---

## Shortcut Tricks

* **The "k" Method:** For equations like $4A = 6B = 10C$, set them equal to $k$ and use $A:B:C = \frac{1}{4} : \frac{1}{6} : \frac{1}{10}$.
* **Why it works:** It converts product-based constraints into a simple ratio by taking the reciprocal of the coefficients.
* **Ratio Scaling:** If you get a ratio like $\frac{1}{6} : \frac{1}{9} : \frac{1}{2}$, multiply by the LCM (18) to get $3:2:9$ instantly.

---

## Common Mistakes

* **Incorrect Dependency:** Misreading "B is 2/3 of C" as "C is 2/3 of B."
    * *Reason:* Rushing through the comparative language.
    * *Fix:* Write the equation explicitly (e.g., $B = \frac{2}{3}C$) before assigning $x$.
* **Summing Variables:** Adding $x$ to the ratio parts instead of the coefficients.
    * *Reason:* Confusing the variable $x$ with the ratio units.
    * *Fix:* Ensure the ratio is fully simplified to integers before calculating the share.
* **Ignoring Time:** Assuming all investments are for the same duration.
    * *Reason:* Overlooking fractions of time mentioned in the problem.
    * *Fix:* Always check if the problem specifies different time periods (Capital $\times$ Time).

---

## Similar Patterns

This pattern is distinct because it relies on algebraic substitution. It is often confused with "Fixed Investment" problems, but you can tell them apart by checking if the problem gives absolute values (e.g., ₹ 5000) or relative values (e.g., "twice as much").

---

## Revision Summary

**Key formula:** $\text{Share} = \text{Total Profit} \times \frac{\text{Partner Ratio}}{\text{Total Ratio}}$.

**Spot it by:** Comparative phrases like "times," "fraction of," or "less than."

**Fastest method:** Assign $x$ to the last person in the dependency chain and work backward.

**Biggest trap:** Misinterpreting the direction of the "times" or "fraction" relationship.
