# Missing Capital: Profit Rate Change

## Overview
* This pattern involves finding an individual's capital when a change in the overall profit percentage leads to a specific monetary increase in that individual's share.
* The central idea is that the ratio of profit shares is identical to the ratio of capitals, allowing you to link the change in total profit to the change in an individual's income.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "rate of profit rises from X% to Y%", "income of [Name] will increase by [Amount]".
* **Given:** Profit share fractions/ratios, the percentage change in total profit, and the absolute increase in one partner's income.
* **Asked:** The total capital of the business or the capital of a specific partner.

---

## Important Formulas

### [Profit Share Equality]
$$
\text{Individual Profit} = (\text{Individual's Share Fraction}) \times (\text{Total Profit Percentage} \times \text{Total Capital})
$$
*Meaning:* An individual's income is their fractional share of the total profit generated by the business.
*Use when:* Calculating the impact of a percentage change on a specific partner's earnings.

---

## Solution Framework
1. Determine the fractional share of profit for the partner mentioned in the problem.
2. Calculate the difference in the profit percentage (e.g., $Y\% - X\%$).
3. Set up the equation: $(\text{Partner's Share Fraction}) \times (\text{Difference in \%}) \times (\text{Total Capital}) = \text{Increase in Income}$.
4. Solve for the total capital ($x$).
5. Multiply the total capital by the partner's share fraction to find their specific capital if requested.

---

## Shortcut Tricks
* No specific shortcut exists; the algebraic framework provided is the most direct method to solve this pattern.

---

## Common Mistakes
* **Mistake:** Applying the percentage increase to the partner's capital instead of the total capital.
    * *Reason:* Confusing the base of the percentage (total profit vs. individual investment).
    * *Fix:* Always apply the percentage change to the total capital ($x$) first, then multiply by the partner's share fraction.
* **Mistake:** Forgetting to calculate the partner's share fraction correctly when multiple partners are involved.
    * *Reason:* Misinterpreting "remainder" or "equally" in the problem statement.
    * *Fix:* Explicitly write out the share fraction for each partner before starting the calculation.

---

## Similar Patterns
* This pattern is distinct from standard partnership problems because it involves a variable profit rate rather than a fixed profit amount.

---

## Revision Summary
**Key formula:** $\text{Partner's Share} \times \Delta\% \times \text{Total Capital} = \Delta\text{Income}$.
**Spot it by:** Looking for a change in profit percentage and a specific dollar/rupee increase for one partner.
**Fastest method:** Solve for total capital ($x$) first using the partner's share fraction, then derive individual capital.
**Biggest trap:** Applying the percentage change directly to the individual's capital instead of the total business capital.
