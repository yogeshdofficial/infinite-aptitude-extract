# Relative Motion: Gap-Crossing Pattern

## Overview
This pattern involves two objects moving towards each other that start at a specific distance apart, cross each other, and end up at that same distance apart again. The central idea is that the total distance covered by the objects is the sum of the initial gap and the final gap.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "opposite directions," "distance between them is X," "distance between them is again X," "after T hours."
* **Given:** Two speeds ($S_1, S_2$), initial gap ($D_{initial}$), and final gap ($D_{final}$).
* **Asked:** Time ($T$) taken to reach the final gap position.

---

## Key Formulas

### [Relative Speed]
$$
S_{rel} = S_1 + S_2
$$
**Variables:** $S_1, S_2$ = speeds of the two objects.
**When to use:** Whenever two objects move in opposite directions.
**Worked example:** $S_1 = 75$ km/hr, $S_2 = 100$ km/hr. $S_{rel} = 75 + 100 = 175$ km/hr.

### [Total Distance]
$$
D_{total} = D_{initial} + D_{final}
$$
**Variables:** $D_{initial}$ = starting gap, $D_{final}$ = target gap after crossing.
**When to use:** To find the total distance covered by the objects relative to each other.
**Worked example:** $D_{initial} = 100$ km, $D_{final} = 100$ km. $D_{total} = 100 + 100 = 200$ km.

---

## Solution Framework

1. **Step 1: Calculate Relative Speed** — Add the two speeds together because they are moving towards each other.
2. **Step 2: Calculate Total Distance** — Add the initial gap and the final gap to find the total distance the objects must cover relative to each other.
3. **Step 3: Calculate Time** — Divide the total distance by the relative speed ($T = \frac{D_{total}}{S_{rel}}$).

---

## Shortcut Tricks

* **Trick:** If $D_{initial} = D_{final}$, the total distance is simply $2 \times D_{initial}$.
* **Why it works:** The objects must close the first gap to meet, then open the same gap again.
* **When to use it:** When the question states the distance is the same before and after crossing.
* **Example:** $D_{initial} = 100$, so $D_{total} = 2 \times 100 = 200$ km.

---

## Common Mistakes

* **Mistake:** Assuming the distance covered is only the initial 100 km.
    * **Why it happens:** Forgetting that the objects must move *past* each other to create the gap on the other side.
    * **Fix:** Always add the initial and final gaps.
* **Mistake:** Subtracting speeds instead of adding.
    * **Why it happens:** Confusing opposite direction motion with same direction motion.
    * **Fix:** Opposite directions = Add speeds; Same direction = Subtract speeds.

---

## Worked Example (Step-by-Step)

**Question:** Two trains are coming from opposite directions with speeds of 75 km/hr and 100 km/hr on two parallel tracks. At some moment the distance between them is 100 km. After T hours, distance between them is again 100 km. T is equal to?

**Solution:**
1. **Relative Speed:** $75 + 100 = 175$ km/hr.
2. **Total Distance:** $100$ (initial gap) $+ 100$ (final gap) $= 200$ km.
3. **Time:** $T = \frac{200}{175} = \frac{8}{7} = 1\frac{1}{7}$ hours.

**Answer:** $1\frac{1}{7}$ hours.

---

## Similar Patterns

**Relative Motion (Same Direction):** Distinguish by the phrase "same direction," which requires subtracting speeds ($S_1 - S_2$) instead of adding them.

---

## Revision Summary

* **Key formula:** $T = \frac{D_{initial} + D_{final}}{S_1 + S_2}$.
* **Spot it by:** "Distance is X, then distance is again X."
* **First move:** Add the two speeds.
* **Fastest method:** Total distance is $2 \times$ gap if the gap is the same.
* **Biggest trap:** Only using the initial distance instead of adding the final distance.

## Appendix: Prerequisites

### Concepts You Must Know
* **Relative Speed** — The speed of one object as observed from another; for opposite directions, it is the sum of individual speeds.
* **Distance-Speed-Time Relationship** — The fundamental equation $D = S \times T$.

### Formulas You Must Know First
$$
\text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}}
$$
**What it means:** Speed is the rate at which distance is covered over time.
**Example:** If a train covers 200 km in 2 hours, its speed is $100$ km/hr.

### Terms Used In This Pattern
* **Relative Speed** — The combined speed of two moving bodies.
* **Opposite Directions** — Moving towards each other from different points.
