# Basic Average

## Overview
This pattern involves calculating the arithmetic mean of a set of values. You recognize it when asked for the "average," "mean," or "average expenditure" of a given list of numbers or items.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "Find the average," "What is the arithmetic mean," "Average body weight," "Average expenditure."
* **Given:** A list of individual values, or a range (e.g., "first 40 natural numbers").
* **Asked:** The single value representing the central tendency of the set.

---

## Key Formulas

### [Arithmetic Mean]
$$
\text{Average} = \frac{\text{Sum of observations}}{\text{Number of observations}}
$$

**Variables:**
- $\text{Sum of observations}$ = The total of all values in the set.
- $\text{Number of observations}$ = The total count of items in the set.

**When to use:** When you have a list of specific values and need the average.

**Worked example:** For weights 54, 64, 75, 67, 45, 91 (6 boys):
Sum = $54+64+75+67+45+91 = 396$.
Average = $\frac{396}{6} = 66$.

### [Sum of First $n$ Natural Numbers]
$$
\text{Sum} = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}
$$

**Variables:**
- $n$ = The last number in the sequence (e.g., 40).

**When to use:** When asked for the average of the "first $n$ natural numbers."

**Worked example:** For first 40 numbers:
Sum = $\frac{40(41)}{2} = 820$.
Average = $\frac{820}{40} = 20.5$.

---

## Solution Framework
1. **Identify the set:** List all values provided in the question.
2. **Count the items:** Determine the total number of items ($n$) in the set.
3. **Calculate the sum:** Add all values together (use formulas for sequences if applicable).
4. **Divide:** Divide the total sum by the count ($n$) to get the average.

---

## Shortcut Tricks
* **Trick:** For an Arithmetic Progression (AP), Average = $\frac{\text{First term} + \text{Last term}}{2}$.
* **Why it works:** In a symmetric sequence, the mean is exactly the middle value.
* **When to use:** When the numbers have a constant difference (e.g., first 20 multiples of 7).
* **Example:** First 20 multiples of 7 are 7 and 140. Average = $\frac{7+140}{2} = 73.5$.

---

## Common Mistakes
* **Mistake:** Miscounting the number of items.
    * **Why it happens:** Skipping a value or misinterpreting the range.
    * **Fix:** Physically tick off each number as you add it.
* **Mistake:** Including 0 or starting from the wrong number.
    * **Why it happens:** Confusion between "first $n$ natural numbers" and "first $n$ whole numbers."
    * **Fix:** Always verify the start and end points of your sequence.

---

## Worked Example (Step-by-Step)

**Question:** Find the average of all prime numbers between 30 and 50.

**Solution:**
1. **Identify the set:** Prime numbers between 30 and 50 are 31, 37, 41, 43, 47.
2. **Count the items:** There are 5 numbers.
3. **Calculate the sum:** $31 + 37 + 41 + 43 + 47 = 199$.
4. **Divide:** $\frac{199}{5} = 39.8$.

**Answer:** 39.8

---

## Similar Patterns
* **Weighted Average:** Distinguished by having different frequencies for values (e.g., "5 shirts at ₹450, 4 trousers at ₹750"). Use total cost divided by total items.

---

## Revision Summary
* **Key formula:** $\text{Average} = \frac{\text{Sum}}{\text{Count}}$.
* **Spot it by:** Looking for "average" or "mean" in the question.
* **First move:** Sum all values carefully.
* **Fastest method:** Use $\frac{\text{First} + \text{Last}}{2}$ for sequences.
* **Biggest trap:** Miscounting the number of items in the set.

## Appendix: Prerequisites

### Concepts You Must Know
* **Arithmetic Mean** — The sum of values divided by the count.
* **Prime Number** — A number greater than 1 divisible only by 1 and itself.
* **Natural Numbers** — Counting numbers starting from 1, 2, 3...

### Formulas You Must Know First
$$
\text{Sum of first } n \text{ natural numbers} = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}
$$
**What it means:** Quickly finds the total of a sequence from 1 to $n$.
**Example:** Sum of 1 to 10 = $\frac{10 \times 11}{2} = 55$.

### Terms Used In This Pattern
* **Observations** — The individual data points or values in a set.
* **Arithmetic Mean** — The formal mathematical term for "average."
