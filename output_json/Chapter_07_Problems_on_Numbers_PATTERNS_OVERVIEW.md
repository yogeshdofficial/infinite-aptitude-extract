# Problems On Numbers — Chapter Overview

## What This Chapter Is About

This chapter focuses on translating word-based puzzles into mathematical equations. It deals with finding unknown quantities by identifying relationships—such as sums, differences, products, or ratios—between numbers. You will learn how to convert descriptive statements into algebraic expressions and solve them to find specific values, ranging from simple integers to fractions and digits.

---

## Core Concepts

**[Algebraic Translation]** — This is the process of converting English sentences into mathematical language. You must learn to treat "is" as an equals sign ($=$), "sum" as addition ($+$), and "exceeds" as a difference ($-$). For example, "a number is 5 more than another" becomes $x = y + 5$.

**[Variable Assignment]** — This involves choosing the most efficient way to represent unknown values. If a problem mentions two numbers, you can represent them as $x$ and $y$, or if their sum is known (e.g., 100), you can represent them as $x$ and $(100 - x)$ to reduce the number of variables.

**[Equation Balancing]** — Every problem in this chapter is essentially a balance scale. Once you translate the words into an equation, you must perform the same operations on both sides of the equals sign to isolate the variable. For example, if $3x + 5 = 20$, you subtract 5 from both sides before dividing by 3.

**[Quadratic Relationships]** — Some problems involve the product or squares of numbers, which lead to equations where the variable is squared ($x^2$). You must be comfortable with factoring these equations or using the quadratic formula to find the two possible values for $x$.

---

## Key Terms Glossary

**Reciprocal** — The multiplicative inverse of a number. For any number $x$, its reciprocal is $\frac{1}{x}$. For example, the reciprocal of $6$ is $\frac{1}{6}$, and the reciprocal of $\frac{2}{3}$ is $\frac{3}{2}$.

**Consecutive Numbers** — A sequence of numbers that follow each other in order without gaps. Consecutive integers are $x, x+1, x+2$. Consecutive even or odd numbers are $x, x+2, x+4$.

**Digit** — The individual symbols used to write numbers ($0, 1, 2, \dots, 9$). In a two-digit number, the value is represented as $10x + y$, where $x$ is the tens digit and $y$ is the units digit.

**Rational Number** — Any number that can be expressed as a fraction $\frac{p}{q}$ where $p$ and $q$ are integers and $q \neq 0$.

## Pattern Map

1. **Linear Equations** (47 questions) — The foundation; involves solving for a single variable using basic arithmetic operations.
2. **Consecutive Numbers** (5 questions) — Defined by a fixed gap (usually 1 or 2) between numbers in a sequence.
3. **Fraction Problems** (13 questions) — Involves relationships between the numerator and denominator or parts of a whole.
4. **Digit Problems** (15 questions) — Requires expressing a number as $10x + y$ based on its place value.
5. **Ratio and Proportion** (7 questions) — Uses a common multiplier ($x$) to represent numbers as parts of a ratio (e.g., $3x, 2x, 5x$).
6. **Reciprocal Relations** (10 questions) — Involves the sum or difference of a number and its inverse ($\frac{1}{x}$).
7. **Algebraic Identities** (26 questions) — Uses standard expansions like $(x+y)^2$ or $(x-y)^2$ to solve for sums and products.
8. **Uncategorized** (1 questions) — Miscellaneous puzzles that do not fit standard algebraic categories.

## Core Formulas

### [Difference of Squares]

$$
x^2 - y^2 = (x + y)(x - y)
$$

**Variables:**
- $x, y$ = the two unknown numbers.

**When to use:** When you know the sum and the difference of two numbers and need to find their squares, or vice versa.

**Key insight:** This is the fastest way to relate the sum and difference of two numbers without solving for them individually.

**Worked example:** If $x+y=10$ and $x-y=4$, then $x^2-y^2 = 10 \times 4 = 40$.

### [Two-Digit Number Representation]

$$
N = 10x + y
$$

**Variables:**
- $x$ = tens digit, $y$ = units digit.

**When to use:** Any problem involving the digits of a two-digit number.

**Key insight:** Remember that reversing the digits results in $10y + x$, not $yx$.

**Worked example:** For the number 47, $x=4$ and $y=7$, so $10(4) + 7 = 47$.

## Suggested Study Order

1. **Linear Equations** — Start here because every other pattern is simply a more complex version of these basic equations.
2. **Consecutive Numbers** — Study next as they rely purely on linear setup and basic addition.
3. **Fraction Problems** — These build on linear equations by introducing division and denominators.
4. **Ratio and Proportion** — These introduce the concept of a common multiplier, which simplifies multi-variable problems.
5. **Digit Problems** — These require a specific understanding of place value notation ($10x+y$).
6. **Reciprocal Relations** — These introduce quadratic forms ($x + \frac{1}{x}$) which are more advanced.
7. **Algebraic Identities** — Study last as they require memorizing specific expansions to solve for sums and products efficiently.

## Chapter-Wide Traps

**Ignoring the "Negative" Root:** When solving quadratic equations, you often get two answers; ensure you check if the problem specifies "natural numbers" or "positive integers" to discard negative results.

**Misinterpreting "Exceeds":** Students often subtract in the wrong order; "A exceeds B by 5" means $A - B = 5$, not $B - A = 5$.

**Place Value Error:** In digit problems, treating a two-digit number $xy$ as $x \times y$ instead of $10x + y$ is the most common cause of failure.
