# Combined Ratio

## Overview
This pattern involves linking multiple separate ratios (e.g., $A:B$ and $B:C$) into a single continuous ratio ($A:B:C$). The central idea is to equate the "bridge" variable (the term common to both ratios) by finding a common multiple.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Given:** Two or more ratios with overlapping variables (e.g., $A:B$ and $B:C$).
* **Asked:** A combined ratio (e.g., $A:B:C$) or a ratio between non-adjacent terms (e.g., $A:C$).
* **Format:** Often presented as "If $A:B = x:y$ and $B:C = z:w$, find $A:B:C$."
* **Fractions:** Ratios may be given as fractions (e.g., $\frac{2}{9} : \frac{1}{3}$); these must be simplified first.

---

## Key Formulas

### [Ratio Multiplication]
$$
\frac{A}{C} = \frac{A}{B} \times \frac{B}{C}
$$
**Variables:** $A, B, C$ are quantities.
**When to use:** When asked for the ratio of the first and last terms in a chain.
**Worked example:** Given $A:B = 3:4$ and $B:C = 5:7$. To find $A:C$: $\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{5}{7} = \frac{15}{28}$, so $A:C = 15:28$.

---

## Solution Framework

**Step 1: Simplify** — Convert all fractional ratios into simple integer ratios by multiplying by the LCM of the denominators.
**Step 2: Identify the Bridge** — Locate the variable that appears in both ratios (e.g., $B$ in $A:B$ and $B:C$).
**Step 3: Equate the Bridge** — Find the LCM of the two values assigned to the bridge variable and multiply both ratios to make the bridge value equal.
**Step 4: Combine** — Write the ratios as a single sequence (e.g., $A:B:C$).
**Step 5: Simplify Final Ratio** — Divide all terms by their greatest common divisor (GCD) if necessary.

---

## Shortcut Tricks

* **Trick:** The "N" Method (for $A:B$ and $B:C$).
* **Why it works:** It cross-multiplies the terms to automatically equate the middle variable.
* **When to use:** When you only have two ratios ($A:B$ and $B:C$) and need $A:B:C$.
* **Example:** $A:B = 3:4$, $B:C = 5:7$. $A:B:C = (3 \times 5) : (4 \times 5) : (4 \times 7) = 15 : 20 : 28$.

---

## Common Mistakes

* **Mistake:** Adding ratios instead of multiplying or equating.
* **Why it happens:** Confusing ratio combination with simple addition.
* **Fix:** Always treat ratios as fractions; use multiplication to link them.
* **Mistake:** Forgetting to invert $D:C$ to $C:D$.
* **Why it happens:** Not checking the order of variables in the given ratio.
* **Fix:** Always align the bridge variable (e.g., ensure $B$ is at the end of the first ratio and start of the second).

---

## Worked Example (Step-by-Step)

**Question:** If $a:b = 2:3$, $b:c = 4:5$, and $c:d = 6:7$, find $a:b:c:d$.

**Solution:**
1. **Identify Bridge:** $b$ is common to first two, $c$ is common to last two.
2. **Combine $a:b$ and $b:c$:** LCM of $b$ (3 and 4) is 12.
   $a:b = 8:12$; $b:c = 12:15$. So, $a:b:c = 8:12:15$.
3. **Combine with $c:d$:** LCM of $c$ (15 and 6) is 30.
   Multiply $a:b:c$ by 2: $16:24:30$.
   Multiply $c:d$ by 5: $30:35$.
4. **Final Result:** $16:24:30:35$.

**Answer:** $16:24:30:35$

## Similar Patterns
**Proportion Problems:** Distinguished by the presence of four terms where the product of means equals the product of extremes ($a:b::c:d$); use this when asked for a missing fourth proportional.

---

## Revision Summary
* **Key formula:** $\frac{A}{C} = \frac{A}{B} \times \frac{B}{C}$.
* **Spot it by:** Overlapping variables in multiple ratios.
* **First move:** Simplify fractional ratios to integers.
* **Fastest method:** Equate the bridge variable using LCM.
* **Biggest trap:** Failing to reorder ratios (e.g., $d:c$ to $c:d$).

---

## Appendix: Prerequisites

### Concepts You Must Know
* **Ratio:** A comparison of two quantities by division, written as $a:b$ or $\frac{a}{b}$.
* **LCM (Least Common Multiple):** The smallest positive integer that is divisible by both numbers.
* **Simplifying Fractions:** Dividing both numerator and denominator by their GCD.

### Formulas You Must Know First
* **Fractional Ratio:** $a:b = \frac{a}{b}$.
* **Equating Ratios:** If $a:b = x:y$, then $a:b = (x \cdot k) : (y \cdot k)$ for any constant $k$.

### Terms Used In This Pattern
* **Antecedent:** The first term of a ratio.
* **Consequent:** The second term of a ratio.
* **Bridge Variable:** The variable that appears in two different ratios, allowing them to be linked.
