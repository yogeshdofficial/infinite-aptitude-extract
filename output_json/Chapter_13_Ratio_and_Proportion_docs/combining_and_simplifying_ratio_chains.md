# Pattern: Combining and Simplifying Ratio Chains

## 1. Pattern Overview
This pattern provides a systematic method to merge multiple individual ratios (e.g., $a:b$ and $b:c$) into a single continuous ratio (e.g., $a:b:c$) to allow for direct comparison or calculation. It is essential for solving problems where quantities are linked through common terms.

## 2. Key Formulas
The primary objective is to equate the "bridge" term (the common variable) across ratios.

**Equating the Bridge:**
If given $a:b = x:y$ and $b:c = m:n$, the combined ratio $a:b:c$ is found by:
$$a:b:c = (x \cdot m) : (y \cdot m) : (y \cdot n)$$
*Where $y$ and $m$ are adjusted to make the $b$ term identical.*

**Compounded Ratio:**
For multiple ratios $(a:b), (c:d), (e:f)$:
$$\text{Compounded Ratio} = (a \cdot c \cdot e) : (b \cdot d \cdot f)$$

## 3. When to Use This Pattern
*   **Keywords:** "Find the ratio of A to C," "If A:B is X and B:C is Y, find A:B:C," "Combined ratio."
*   **Recognition:** You are given two or more ratios that share a common variable (e.g., $A:B$ and $B:C$).
*   **Given:** Two or more separate ratios with at least one overlapping term.
*   **To Find:** A unified ratio representing the relationship between all variables simultaneously.

## 4. Core Concept & Theory
The fundamental idea is **normalization**. A ratio represents a relative value, not an absolute one. To combine $a:b$ and $b:c$, the value assigned to $b$ must be the same in both ratios. By finding the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of the two values representing $b$, we can scale both ratios so that the "bridge" term matches, allowing us to link the ratios into a single chain.

## 5. Step-by-Step Solution Method
1.  **Identify the Bridge:** Locate the variable that appears in both ratios.
2.  **Find the LCM:** Determine the Least Common Multiple of the values assigned to the bridge variable in the two ratios.
3.  **Scale the Ratios:** Multiply each term in the first ratio by a factor to make the bridge equal to the LCM. Do the same for the second ratio.
4.  **Merge:** Write the new ratios as a single chain $a:b:c$.
5.  **Simplify:** Divide all terms by their Greatest Common Divisor (GCD) if the resulting ratio can be reduced further.

## 6. Worked Examples

**Example 1: Combining two ratios**
Given: $A:B = 2:3$ and $B:C = 4:5$. Find $A:B:C$.
Solution:
1. Bridge is $B$. Values are 3 and 4.
2. LCM of 3 and 4 is 12.
3. Scale $A:B$ by 4: $(2 \times 4) : (3 \times 4) = 8:12$.
4. Scale $B:C$ by 3: $(4 \times 3) : (5 \times 3) = 12:15$.
5. Combine: $A:B:C = 8:12:15$.

**Example 2: Compounded Ratio**
Given: Find the compounded ratio of $2:3$, $4:5$, and $6:7$.
Solution:
1. Use the formula $(a \cdot c \cdot e) : (b \cdot d \cdot f)$.
2. Calculation: $(2 \times 4 \times 6) : (3 \times 5 \times 7)$.
3. Result: $48 : 105$.
4. Simplify by dividing by 3: $16 : 35$.

## 7. Recognition Clues & Keywords
*   **Clues:** Look for "linked" variables. If a problem mentions $A$ relative to $B$, and $B$ relative to $C$, you are dealing with a ratio chain.
*   **Distinction:** Do not confuse this with **Proportion** ($a:b :: c:d$), which deals with the equality of two ratios rather than linking them into a chain.

## 8. Common Mistakes
1.  **Direct Concatenation:** Students often write $A:B:C$ as $2:3:5$ (using the original numbers). This is wrong because $B$ is not 3 and 4 simultaneously.
2.  **Forgetting to Simplify:** Always check if the final combined ratio has a common factor.
3.  **Scaling Only One Side:** Students often multiply the bridge term but forget to multiply the other term in the ratio by the same factor.
4.  **Misidentifying the Bridge:** Ensure the variable you are equating is actually the same variable in both ratios.

## 9. Practice Tips
*   **Master the LCM:** Speed in finding the LCM of the bridge terms is the key to solving these problems quickly.
*   **Verification:** Once you have the combined ratio, check if the sub-ratios (e.g., $A:B$) still hold true. If $8:12$ simplifies to $2:3$, your work is correct.
*   **Related Patterns:** Study **Variation** (Section VI) as it often involves ratio chains in physics and chemistry problems.
