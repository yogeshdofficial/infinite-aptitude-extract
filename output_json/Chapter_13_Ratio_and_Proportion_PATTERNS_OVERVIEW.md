# Chapter 13: Ratio and Proportion

## 1. Chapter Introduction
This chapter explores the mathematical relationships between quantities. By studying ratios and proportions, you will learn how to compare values, scale measurements, and solve for unknown variables in equations. These concepts are fundamental tools for solving real-world problems involving mixtures, cost distributions, and proportional variations.

## 2. Core Ideas
*   **Ratio:** A way to compare two quantities ($a$ and $b$) of the same unit, written as $a:b$. The first term is the *antecedent* and the second is the *consequent*.
*   **Proportion:** This occurs when two ratios are equal ($a:b = c:d$). In this state, the product of the means ($b \times c$) must equal the product of the extremes ($a \times d$).
*   **Variation:** Describes how one quantity changes in relation to another, either directly ($x = ky$) or inversely ($xy = k$).

## 3. Important Formulas
*   **Proportionality Rule:** 
    $$a : b :: c : d \iff (b \times c) = (a \times d)$$
    *Use this to solve for an unknown variable when two ratios are equated.*
*   **Compounded Ratio:** 
    $$\text{Compounded ratio of } (a:b), (c:d), (e:f) = (ace : bdf)$$
    *Use this when combining multiple ratios into one.*
*   **Componendo and Dividendo:** 
    $$\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d} \iff \frac{a+b}{a-b} = \frac{c+d}{c-d}$$
    *Use this to simplify complex algebraic expressions involving ratios.*
*   **Mixture Replacement:** 
    $$\text{Final liquid} = x \left(1 - \frac{y}{x}\right)^n$$
    *Use this to find the remaining quantity of pure liquid after $n$ replacement operations.*

## 4. Important Facts and Shortcuts
*   **Scaling:** Multiplying or dividing both terms of a ratio by the same non-zero number does not change the ratio (e.g., $4:5 = 8:10$).
*   **Proportional Types:**
    *   **Fourth Proportional:** $d$ in $a:b = c:d$.
    *   **Third Proportional:** $c$ in $a:b = b:c$.
    *   **Mean Proportional:** $\sqrt{ab}$.
*   **Powers:** You can find duplicate ($a^2:b^2$) or triplicate ($a^3:b^3$) ratios by raising the terms to the respective powers.

## 5. How to Recognize the Problem Type
*   **"Find the value of $x$":** Look for equality between two ratios; use the product of means equals product of extremes rule.
*   **"Directly/Inversely proportional":** Use the variation formulas ($x=ky$ or $xy=k$).
*   **"Percentage of $x$ equals percentage of $y$":** Convert percentages to fractions and set them equal to solve for the ratio $x:y$.
*   **"Mixture/Replacement":** If a problem mentions taking out liquid and replacing it with water multiple times, use the $n$-operation formula.

## 6. Common Mistakes
*   **Units Mismatch:** Attempting to find a ratio between quantities that are not in the same units.
*   **Confusing Means and Extremes:** Mixing up the placement of terms in the $a:b::c:d$ formula, leading to an incorrect product.
*   **Algebraic Errors:** Forgetting to apply operations to both sides of the ratio when simplifying.
*   **Misinterpreting Variation:** Confusing direct variation ($x/y = k$) with inverse variation ($xy = k$).

## 7. Chapter Pattern Map
The chapter covers a wide range of problem-solving patterns, primarily focusing on **solving for variables in proportional equations** (the most frequent pattern). Other key areas include distributing quantities by ratio, combining ratio chains, and evaluating expressions through substitution.
