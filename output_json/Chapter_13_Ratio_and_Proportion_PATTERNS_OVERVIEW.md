# Ratio And Proportion — Chapter Overview

## What This Chapter Is About

This chapter deals with the mathematical relationship between quantities of the same kind, expressed as a comparison of their relative sizes. The central theme is the "ratio," which acts as a scaling factor that allows us to compare, distribute, or adjust values even when the actual totals are unknown. You will encounter problems ranging from simple comparisons and proportional distributions to complex scenarios involving mixtures, income-expenditure balancing, and data analysis.

---

## Core Concepts

**[The Concept of Scaling]** — A ratio represents a relationship, not an absolute value. If the ratio of two quantities is $2:3$, it does not mean the quantities are $2$ and $3$; it means they are some multiple ($2k$ and $3k$) of those numbers. This "multiplier" ($k$) is the key to unlocking almost every problem in this chapter.

**[Equivalence of Ratios]** — Ratios remain unchanged if both terms are multiplied or divided by the same non-zero number. For example, $4:6$ is identical to $2:3$ because both terms share a common factor of $2$. Always simplify your ratios to their lowest terms before performing calculations to keep the numbers manageable.

**[Proportionality]** — A proportion is simply an equality between two ratios ($a:b = c:d$). This concept allows you to find a missing fourth value if you know the relationship between the first three. It is the foundation for solving problems where one variable changes in response to another.

**[Combined Ratios]** — When you have multiple ratios involving shared terms (e.g., $A:B$ and $B:C$), you must "bridge" them to create a single ratio ($A:B:C$). This is essential for distributing a total amount across three or more entities.

**[Variation]** — This describes how two quantities move in relation to each other. In "direct variation," as one increases, the other increases at the same rate ($x = ky$). In "inverse variation," as one increases, the other decreases ($xy = k$).

## Key Terms Glossary

**Antecedent** — The first term in a ratio ($a$ in $a:b$).

**Consequent** — The second term in a ratio ($b$ in $a:b$).

**Extremes** — The outer terms in a proportion ($a$ and $d$ in $a:b::c:d$).

**Means** — The inner terms in a proportion ($b$ and $c$ in $a:b::c:d$).

**Duplicate Ratio** — The square of the terms of a ratio ($a^2:b^2$).

**Compounded Ratio** — The product of multiple ratios, calculated by multiplying all antecedents together and all consequents together ($\frac{a}{b} \times \frac{c}{d} \times \frac{e}{f} = \frac{ace}{bdf}$).

## Core Formulas

### [Proportion Property]
$$
\text{If } a:b :: c:d, \text{ then } b \times c = a \times d
$$
**Variables:** $a, b, c, d$ are the four terms of the proportion.
**When to use:** When you need to find a missing value in a proportion.
**Key insight:** The product of the means always equals the product of the extremes.
**Worked example:** If $2:x :: 4:10$, then $4x = 20$, so $x = 5$.

### [Mixture Replacement]
$$
\text{Final Liquid} = \text{Initial} \times \left(1 - \frac{\text{Removed}}{\text{Total}}\right)^n
$$
**Variables:** $\text{Removed}$ = amount taken out, $\text{Total}$ = container capacity, $n$ = number of operations.
**When to use:** When a liquid is repeatedly removed and replaced with another.
**Key insight:** This formula calculates the *remaining* amount of the original liquid, not the added one.
**Worked example:** 10L removed from 100L, repeated twice: $100 \times (1 - \frac{10}{100})^2 = 81\text{L}$.

## Chapter-Wide Traps

**Confusing Ratio with Value:** Treating the ratio $2:3$ as the actual values $2$ and $3$ instead of $2k$ and $3k$ → Always introduce a constant $k$ when working with totals.

**Ignoring Units:** Attempting to form a ratio between quantities with different units (e.g., kg and grams) → Always convert all quantities to the same unit before forming the ratio.

**Misinterpreting "Inverse":** Assuming $A:B = 2:3$ means $A$ is $2$ and $B$ is $3$ in inverse variation problems → Remember that in inverse variation, the product is constant, not the ratio.
