# Partnership

## What This Chapter Is About

Partnership deals with the distribution of profits or losses among individuals who contribute capital to a joint business venture. Every problem in this chapter revolves around the fundamental relationship that profit is directly proportional to the product of the capital invested and the time for which it remains invested.

---

## Key Terms

**Partner** — An individual who contributes capital to a business venture.

**Capital** — The amount of money invested by a partner into the business.

**Time Period** — The duration for which a specific amount of capital is invested.

**Simple Partnership** — A business where all partners invest their capital for the same duration; profit is shared in the ratio of investments.

**Compound Partnership** — A business where partners invest capital for different durations; profit is shared in the ratio of `Capital × Time`.

**Working Partner** — A partner who receives an additional share of profit as compensation for managing the business operations.

**Sleeping Partner** — A partner who contributes capital but does not participate in the day-to-day management of the business.

---

## Core Formulas

### [Profit Distribution Ratio]

$$
\text{Profit}_A : \text{Profit}_B : \text{Profit}_C = (C_1 \times T_1) : (C_2 \times T_2) : (C_3 \times T_3)
$$

*When to use:* To determine how to divide total profit among partners based on their respective investments and time durations.

*Variables:* $C$ = Capital invested; $T$ = Time period of investment.

### [Equivalent Capital]

$$
\text{Equivalent Capital} = \text{Capital} \times \text{Time Units}
$$

*When to use:* To normalize investments when partners contribute money for different lengths of time.

*Variables:* Capital = Amount invested; Time Units = Number of months or years the capital was active.

## How Patterns Connect

*   **Capital Changes** — distinct because the investment amount varies for a single partner during the business term.
*   **Missing Capital** — distinct because the total profit and time are known, but one or more initial investment amounts are unknown.
*   **Missing Capital: Profit Rate Change** — distinct because the distribution ratio is altered by a specific change in profit-sharing terms.
*   **Missing Initial Capital** — distinct because the final profit distribution is provided, requiring reverse calculation of the starting investment.
*   **Missing Initial Capital: Investment Ratio Change** — distinct because the ratio of investments is modified mid-term, affecting the final profit share.
*   **Missing Time** — distinct because the capital amounts are known, but the duration of one partner's investment is the unknown variable.
*   **Missing Total Profit** — distinct because individual shares are known, and the goal is to find the aggregate sum.
*   **Mixed Distribution** — distinct because it involves multiple, non-standard adjustments to how profit is allocated.
*   **Ratio of Investment** — distinct because the final profit ratio is given, and the goal is to find the ratio of the capitals.
*   **Ratio of Time** — distinct because the capital and profit ratios are given, requiring the calculation of the time ratio.
*   **Relative Investment** — distinct because investments are defined as fractions or multiples of one another rather than absolute values.
*   **Rent Sharing** — distinct because the "profit" is actually a cost (rent) shared based on usage of space or resources.
*   **Rent Sharing: Equivalent Units** — distinct because it uses a common unit of measurement to equate different types of resource usage.
*   **Rent Sharing: Phased Usage** — distinct because the rent is calculated based on changing occupancy levels over specific time phases.
*   **Simple Partnership** — distinct because all partners invest for the exact same duration, making time a constant factor.
*   **Varying Time** — distinct because partners join or leave the business at different points throughout the year.
*   **Working Partner** — distinct because a portion of the profit is pre-allocated as a salary or management fee before the remainder is split.

---

## Common Traps (Chapter-Wide)

*   **Ignoring Time Units:** Failing to convert all time periods into the same unit (e.g., mixing months and years) before calculating the product of capital and time.
*   **Miscalculating "Balance" Profit:** In working partner problems, failing to subtract the management fee from the total profit before distributing the remainder according to the capital ratio.
*   **Static Capital Assumption:** Assuming capital remains constant throughout the year when the problem statement specifies withdrawals or additional investments.
