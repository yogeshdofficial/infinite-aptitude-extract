# Partnership — Chapter Overview

## What This Chapter Is About

Partnership deals with the mathematical distribution of profits or losses among individuals who pool their capital to run a business. The central relationship is that profit is always proportional to the product of the capital invested and the duration for which it remains in the business. Problems in this chapter require you to calculate individual shares based on varying investment amounts, different time periods, or specific contractual agreements like salary for working partners.

---

## Core Concepts

**[Investment-Time Product]** — Profit is not determined by money alone, but by "money-at-work." If you invest ₹100 for 12 months, you have contributed the same "weight" to the business as someone who invests ₹1200 for 1 month. You must always multiply the capital by the time to find the true basis for profit sharing.

**[Ratio of Gains]** — The total profit is divided among partners in the exact ratio of their respective investment-time products. If Partner A’s product is 200 and Partner B’s is 300, the profit must be split in a 2:3 ratio, regardless of how much they initially invested.

**[Capital Consistency]** — In simple partnerships, capital remains static. However, in complex scenarios, partners may withdraw or add capital mid-year. You must treat these as separate "blocks" of investment, calculating the product for each period and summing them to find the total contribution.

**[Working vs. Sleeping Partners]** — A business may have a "working partner" who receives a fixed salary or a percentage of the profit for management duties before the remaining profit is distributed. You must subtract this management fee from the total profit *before* applying the investment ratio to the balance.

---

## Key Terms Glossary

**Capital** — The amount of money invested by a partner into the business.

**Simple Partnership** — A business arrangement where all partners invest their capital for the exact same duration of time.

**Compound Partnership** — A business arrangement where partners invest their capital for different durations, or change their investment amounts during the business term.

**Working Partner** — A partner who manages the day-to-day operations of the business and is typically compensated with a salary or a share of the profit before the standard distribution.

**Sleeping Partner** — A partner who contributes capital but does not participate in the active management of the business.

## Pattern Map

**[Simple Partnership]** (15 questions) — The baseline scenario where all partners invest for the same duration; profit is shared strictly by the ratio of capital.

**[Varying Time]** (29 questions) — Partners join or leave at different times; you must calculate the specific number of months each person's money was active.

**[Capital Changes]** (18 questions) — A partner adds or withdraws money mid-term; you must calculate the weighted average of their capital over the total time.

**[Complex Distribution]** (5 questions) — Involves multiple conditions, such as ratios of ratios or complex fractional investments, requiring algebraic setup before finding the profit ratio.

**[Missing Variable]** (7 questions) — The profit ratio or total profit is known, but one of the inputs (time or capital) is unknown; requires solving for $x$.

**[Working Partner]** (3 questions) — A portion of the profit is set aside for management; you must calculate the "net profit" available for distribution after this deduction.

**[Uncategorized]** (1 question) — Miscellaneous problems that do not fit the standard investment-time-profit framework.

## Core Formulas

### Profit Distribution Ratio
$$
\text{Share of Profit} = \frac{\text{Capital} \times \text{Time}}{\text{Total Product of all Partners}} \times \text{Total Profit}
$$

**Variables:**
- $\text{Capital}$ = Amount invested by the individual.
- $\text{Time}$ = Duration the capital was invested.
- $\text{Total Product}$ = Sum of $(\text{Capital} \times \text{Time})$ for all partners.

**When to use:** Any problem where you need to find the specific monetary share of a partner.

**Key insight:** Always simplify the ratio of products to the smallest whole numbers before multiplying by the total profit to avoid massive calculations.

**Worked example:** If A invests 200 for 6 months and B invests 300 for 4 months, the ratio is $1200:1200$, or $1:1$. Each gets half the profit.

## Suggested Study Order

1. **Simple Partnership** — Start here to master the fundamental concept that profit follows the ratio of investment.
2. **Varying Time** — Study next to understand how to handle different durations, which is the most common variation in exams.
3. **Capital Changes** — Build on the time concept by learning how to handle "blocks" of capital when amounts change mid-year.
4. **Working Partner** — Learn this to handle business-logic deductions (salaries) before applying the ratio.
5. **Complex Distribution & Missing Variable** — Tackle these last as they require strong algebraic manipulation of the previous concepts.

## Chapter-Wide Traps

**Ignoring the Time Unit:** Students often use the investment amount alone, forgetting that money invested for 3 months is worth less than money invested for 12 months → Always multiply every capital amount by its respective time duration.

**Miscalculating "Remaining" Profit:** In working partner problems, students often distribute the *total* profit before subtracting the manager's share → Always subtract the fixed salary or management percentage from the total profit first.

**Ratio Inversion:** Students sometimes invert the ratio (e.g., giving the person with less capital more profit) → Always double-check that the partner with the higher product receives the higher share.
