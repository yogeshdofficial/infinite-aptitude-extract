# Marked Price & Discount

## Overview
These questions involve a chain of three values: Cost Price (C.P.), Marked Price (M.P.), and Selling Price (S.P.). The central idea is to link these values using percentages: $S.P. = M.P. \times (1 - \text{Discount}\%)$ and $S.P. = C.P. \times (1 + \text{Profit}\%)$.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "Marked price," "Discount," "Above cost price," "Labelled price," "Commission."
* **Given:** Usually a relationship between C.P. and M.P. (e.g., "marked 50% above C.P.") and a discount percentage.
* **Asked:** Either the final profit percentage or the required markup percentage to achieve a target profit.
* **Scan for:** Multiple percentage changes applied to a base value (C.P. or M.P.).

---

## Important Formulas

### Selling Price from Marked Price
$$
S.P. = M.P. \times \left(1 - \frac{d}{100}\right)
$$
*Meaning:* $d$ is the discount percentage.
*Use when:* You need to find the actual sale price after a discount is applied to the label.

### Profit Percentage
$$
\text{Profit}\% = \left(\frac{S.P. - C.P.}{C.P.}\right) \times 100
$$
*Meaning:* $S.P.$ is the final price after discount; $C.P.$ is the original cost.
*Use when:* Calculating the final gain after all transactions are complete.

---

## Solution Framework
1. **Assume Base:** Always assume $C.P. = 100$ (or $M.P. = 100$ if the question starts with the label).
2. **Set M.P.:** Calculate $M.P.$ based on the "above cost" percentage given.
3. **Apply Discount:** Calculate $S.P.$ by applying the discount percentage to the $M.P.$
4. **Calculate Profit:** Find the difference between $S.P.$ and $C.P.$ to get the profit percentage.

---

## Shortcut Tricks
* **Successive Percentage Change:** If an item is marked up by $x\%$ and then discounted by $y\%$, the net profit/loss is $(x - y - \frac{xy}{100})\%$.
    * *Why:* This is a direct application of the successive percentage formula for two changes on the same base.
* **Ratio Method:** Write $C.P. : M.P. : S.P.$ as a ratio chain.
    * *Why:* It eliminates the need for "Let $x = 100$" and prevents errors when dealing with complex fractions.

---

## Common Mistakes
* **Discount on C.P.:** Applying the discount to the $C.P.$ instead of the $M.P.$
    * *Reason:* Confusing the base of the percentage.
    * *Fix:* Always remember: Discount is *always* on $M.P.$, Profit is *always* on $C.P.$
* **Adding Percentages:** Simply subtracting the discount from the markup (e.g., 50% markup - 25% discount = 25% profit).
    * *Reason:* Percentages are multiplicative, not additive.
    * *Fix:* Use the $100 \to M.P. \to S.P.$ chain method.

---

## Similar Patterns
This pattern is distinct but often confused with "Successive Discounts" (where multiple discounts are applied to the same M.P.). Remember: In this pattern, the goal is to bridge the gap between $C.P.$ and $S.P.$, whereas successive discounts only focus on the $M.P.$ to $S.P.$ transition.

---

## Revision Summary
**Key formula:** $S.P. = M.P. \times (1 - \text{Discount}\%)$.
**Spot it by:** Keywords "Marked Price," "Discount," and "Profit %."
**Fastest method:** Assume $C.P. = 100$ and follow the chain to $S.P.$
**Biggest trap:** Applying the discount percentage to the $C.P.$ instead of the $M.P.$
