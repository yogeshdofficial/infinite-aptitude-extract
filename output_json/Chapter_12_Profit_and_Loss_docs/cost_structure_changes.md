# Cost Structure Changes

## Overview
These questions involve a product with multiple cost components (raw material, labor, etc.) where individual costs and the selling price undergo percentage changes. The central idea is to assume an initial total cost of ₹100, calculate the new individual costs, and derive the new profit percentage based on the updated selling price.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "Components," "raw material," "manufacturing cost," "production cost," "revised selling price."
* **Given:** Percentage breakdown of initial costs, percentage increase/decrease for each component, and a change in selling price.
* **Asked:** New profit percentage, new selling price, or change in total profit.
* **Structure:** Look for a multi-part cost base (e.g., "A is 10%, B is 20% of production cost").

---

## Important Formulas

### [New Cost Calculation]
$$
\text{New Total Cost} = \sum (\text{Original Component Cost} \times (1 + \text{Change \%}))
$$
*Meaning:* Sum of all individual components after applying their specific percentage changes.
*Use when:* You need to find the updated total cost of production.

### [Profit Percentage]
$$
\text{Profit \%} = \left( \frac{\text{New S.P.} - \text{New Total Cost}}{\text{New Total Cost}} \right) \times 100
$$
*Meaning:* Profit calculated strictly on the new cost and new selling price.
*Use when:* The question asks for the final profit percentage after all changes.

---

## Solution Framework
1. Assume the initial total cost is ₹100.
2. Split ₹100 into individual components based on the given percentages.
3. Apply the percentage change to each component individually to find the new component costs.
4. Calculate the new total cost by summing the updated components.
5. Calculate the new selling price using the given percentage change on the original selling price.
6. Find the profit by subtracting the new total cost from the new selling price.

---

## Shortcut Tricks
* **The ₹100 Base:** Always start with ₹100 for the total cost; it makes all percentage shifts direct additions or subtractions.
* **Ignore "Other" Components:** If the cost of "other" components does not change, simply add the change in the specific components to the original ₹100 base.
* **Ratio Scaling:** If costs are given in ratios (e.g., 3:1), convert them to percentages of the total cost immediately to keep calculations consistent.

---

## Common Mistakes
* **Applying % change to the total:** Applying the increase to the total cost instead of individual components (e.g., increasing the whole ₹100 by 20% instead of just the 10% component).
* **Mixing Bases:** Calculating profit percentage using the *original* cost instead of the *new* cost.
* **S.P. Confusion:** Forgetting that the selling price change is applied to the *original* selling price, not the original cost price.

---

## Similar Patterns
* **False Weight Patterns:** These are distinct because they involve physical weight manipulation rather than cost structure shifts.
* **Successive Percentage Changes:** These involve a single item's price changing multiple times; this pattern is distinct because it involves multiple *independent* components changing simultaneously.

---

## Revision Summary
**Key formula:** $\text{New Cost} = \sum (\text{Original Component} \times \text{Multiplier})$.
**Spot it by:** Multiple cost components with different percentage changes.
**Fastest method:** Assume ₹100 total cost and track individual component shifts.
**Biggest trap:** Applying percentage changes to the total cost instead of individual parts.
