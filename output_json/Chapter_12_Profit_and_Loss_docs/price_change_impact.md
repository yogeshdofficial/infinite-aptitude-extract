# Price Change Impact

## Overview
These questions involve scenarios where the selling price or cost price is modified, resulting in a change in profit or loss percentage. The central idea is to express both the initial and final states as percentages of the Cost Price ($CP$) and equate their difference to the given monetary change.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "If the selling price had been increased/reduced by ₹...", "If he had bought it at... less", "Profit/Loss is equal to...".
* **Given:** Two different selling prices or a change in $CP$ and $SP$ leading to a specific change in profit/loss percentage.
* **Asked:** Usually the original Cost Price ($CP$) or a new Selling Price ($SP$) for a target profit.

---

## Important Formulas

### [Percentage Difference Method]
$$
\text{Difference in \% of } CP = \text{Difference in Amount}
$$
*Meaning:* The absolute difference between two profit/loss percentages (e.g., $+5\%$ and $-20\%$) equals the change in $SP$.
*Use when:* The $CP$ remains constant, but the $SP$ changes.

### [Equating Profit/Loss]
$$
(SP_1 - CP) = k(CP - SP_2)
$$
*Meaning:* $SP_1$ and $SP_2$ are two selling prices; $k$ is the ratio of profit to loss.
*Use when:* The problem states profit at one price is $k$ times the loss at another price.

---

## Solution Framework
1. Assume $CP = 100$ units or $x$.
2. Express the first state as a percentage of $CP$ (e.g., $125\%x$).
3. Express the second state as a percentage of $CP$ (e.g., $130\%$ of $80\%x$).
4. Calculate the difference between the two states.
5. Equate this difference to the given monetary value and solve for $x$.

---

## Shortcut Tricks
* **The "Gap" Trick:** If a price change turns a loss of $a\%$ into a profit of $b\%$, then $CP = \frac{\text{Change in Price}}{a + b} \times 100$.
    * *Why:* The total percentage shift is the sum of the loss and the profit.
* **Equal Profit/Loss:** If profit at $SP_1$ equals loss at $SP_2$, then $CP = \frac{SP_1 + SP_2}{2}$.
    * *Why:* The $CP$ is the exact midpoint between the two selling prices.

---

## Common Mistakes
* **Adding/Subtracting Percentages:** Adding profit and loss percentages incorrectly (e.g., treating $20\%$ loss as $+20\%$ instead of $-20\%$).
    * *Reason:* Confusing absolute values with signed values.
    * *Fix:* Always use $100 \pm \%$ notation (e.g., $80\%$ for $20\%$ loss, $105\%$ for $5\%$ profit).
* **Base Change:** Applying the second percentage to the original $CP$ when the $CP$ itself has changed.
    * *Reason:* Forgetting that "bought at $20\%$ less" creates a new base.
    * *Fix:* Calculate the new $CP$ first, then apply the new profit percentage to that specific value.

---

## Similar Patterns
* **False Weight Patterns:** These are distinct because they involve a ratio of weights rather than a change in monetary price.
* **Two-Item Profit/Loss:** These involve two separate articles; focus on the $CP$ of each individually rather than a single $CP$ variable.

---

## Revision Summary
**Key formula:** $\text{Difference in \% of } CP = \text{Change in Amount}$.
**Spot it by:** Looking for "If the price had been..." or "Profit equals loss" phrases.
**Fastest method:** Use the "Gap" trick for simple price changes and the "Midpoint" rule for equal profit/loss.
**Biggest trap:** Applying a new profit percentage to the original $CP$ after the $CP$ has been modified.
