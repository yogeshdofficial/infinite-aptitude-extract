# Rate-Based Transactions

## Overview
These questions involve buying and selling items at specific rates (e.g., "x items for ₹ y") rather than a single unit price. The central idea is to normalize the quantity of items to a common base (usually the L.C.M. of the quantities) to compare Cost Price (C.P.) and Selling Price (S.P.) directly.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "at the rate of", "x for ₹ y", "bought at... sold at...".
* **Given:** Two different rates for buying and selling, or a rate and a target profit/loss percentage.
* **Asked:** Profit/loss percentage, or the number of items required to achieve a specific monetary profit/loss.
* **Structure:** Often involves "equal number" of items bought at two different rates.

---

## Important Formulas

### [Normalized Price Calculation]
$$
\text{Total Price} = \left( \frac{\text{Price}}{\text{Quantity}} \right) \times \text{Common Quantity}
$$
*Meaning:* Converts a rate into the actual cost/revenue for a specific batch size.
*Use when:* Comparing two different rates to find profit or loss.

### [Profit/Loss Percentage]
$$
\text{Profit/Loss \%} = \left( \frac{\text{S.P.} - \text{C.P.}}{\text{C.P.}} \right) \times 100
$$
*Meaning:* Standard percentage calculation based on the normalized C.P.
*Use when:* The question asks for the gain or loss percentage.

---

## Solution Framework
1. Identify the quantities in the given rates.
2. Calculate the L.C.M. of these quantities to use as the "Common Quantity."
3. Calculate the C.P. for the Common Quantity.
4. Calculate the S.P. for the Common Quantity.
5. Find the difference (Profit/Loss) and calculate the percentage or required quantity.

---

## Shortcut Tricks
* **The Cross-Multiplication Method:** If buying $a$ items for $₹ b$ and selling $c$ items for $₹ d$, the profit/loss is determined by comparing $(b \times c)$ and $(a \times d)$.
* **Why it works:** It effectively calculates the total S.P. and C.P. for a batch size of $(a \times c)$ without explicitly writing out the L.C.M. steps.

---

## Common Mistakes
* **Mixing up Numerator/Denominator:** Placing price over quantity instead of quantity over price.
    * *Reason:* Confusing "rate" with "unit price."
    * *Fix:* Always write the rate as $\frac{\text{Price}}{\text{Quantity}}$ and multiply by the quantity.
* **Ignoring "Equal Number" clause:** Calculating profit on a single item instead of the combined batch.
    * *Reason:* Overlooking the word "equal" in mixed-rate problems.
    * *Fix:* Always assume 1 unit of each batch and sum the C.P.s before comparing to S.P.
* **Calculating % on S.P.:** Using S.P. as the denominator for profit percentage.
    * *Reason:* Habitual error in profit/loss chapters.
    * *Fix:* Always use C.P. as the base for percentage calculations.

---

## Similar Patterns
* **False Weight Problems:** These are distinct because they involve a discrepancy in *quantity* rather than *rate*.
* **Comparison:** Rate problems involve changing prices for fixed quantities; false weight problems involve fixed prices for changing quantities.

---

## Revision Summary
**Key formula:** $\text{Profit/Loss \%} = \frac{\text{S.P.} - \text{C.P.}}{\text{C.P.}} \times 100$.
**Spot it by:** Rates given as "x items for ₹ y".
**Fastest method:** Cross-multiply the price and quantity values.
**Biggest trap:** Calculating percentage profit based on the Selling Price.
