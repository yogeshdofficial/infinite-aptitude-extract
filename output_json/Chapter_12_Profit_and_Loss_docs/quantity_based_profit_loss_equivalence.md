# Pattern: Quantity-Based Profit/Loss (Equivalence)

## 1. Pattern Overview
- This pattern solves problems where the Cost Price (C.P.) of a specific quantity of items is equated to the Selling Price (S.P.) of a different quantity of the same items.
- It matters because it allows traders and students to determine profit or loss percentages without knowing the absolute monetary value of the goods, relying instead on the ratio of quantities.

## 2. Key Formulas
- **Equivalence Principle:** If the C.P. of $x$ articles equals the S.P. of $y$ articles:
$$\text{Gain \%} = \left( \frac{x - y}{y} \right) \times 100$$
- **General Profit/Loss Logic:**
$$\text{Profit} = S.P. - C.P.$$
$$\text{Loss} = C.P. - S.P.$$
$$\text{Profit \%} = \left( \frac{\text{Profit}}{C.P.} \right) \times 100$$
$$\text{Loss \%} = \left( \frac{\text{Loss}}{C.P.} \right) \times 100$$

## 3. When to Use This Pattern
- **Keywords:** "Cost price of X articles equals selling price of Y articles," "If the selling price of 10 pens is equal to the cost price of 12 pens."
- **Recognition Clues:** The problem provides two different quantities and links them via an equality statement.
- **Given:** Two quantities ($x$ and $y$) and the relationship between their C.P. and S.P.
- **To Find:** The net percentage of profit or loss.

## 4. Core Concept & Theory
The fundamental idea is that profit or loss is determined by the difference in quantity exchanged for the same amount of money. If you sell fewer items for the price you paid for more items, you have made a profit. If you sell more items for the price you paid for fewer, you have incurred a loss. We normalize the calculation to a base of 100 to find the percentage.

## 5. Step-by-Step Solution Method
1. **Identify the quantities:** Let $x$ be the quantity for C.P. and $y$ be the quantity for S.P.
2. **Assume a value:** Assume the C.P. of $x$ articles is $1$ unit of currency.
3. **Calculate unit prices:** 
   - $C.P. \text{ of 1 article} = \frac{1}{x}$
   - $S.P. \text{ of 1 article} = \frac{1}{y}$
4. **Determine Profit/Loss:** Compare the unit prices. If $S.P. > C.P.$, it is a profit; if $S.P. < C.P.$, it is a loss.
5. **Calculate Percentage:** Apply the standard percentage formula using the unit values derived in Step 3.

## 6. Worked Examples

**Example 1: Quantity Equivalence**
*Problem: The cost price of 15 articles is equal to the selling price of 10 articles. Find the profit percent.*
Given: $x = 15, y = 10$.
Solution:
1. Since $x > y$ (we sold fewer items for the price of more), it is a profit.
2. Using the formula: $\text{Profit \%} = \left( \frac{15 - 10}{10} \right) \times 100$.
3. $\text{Profit \%} = \left( \frac{5}{10} \right) \times 100 = 50\%$.

**Example 2: Loss Scenario**
*Problem: The cost price of 20 articles is equal to the selling price of 25 articles. Find the loss percent.*
Given: $x = 20, y = 25$.
Solution:
1. Since $x < y$ (we sold more items for the price of fewer), it is a loss.
2. Using the formula: $\text{Loss \%} = \left( \frac{25 - 20}{25} \right) \times 100$.
3. $\text{Loss \%} = \left( \frac{5}{25} \right) \times 100 = 20\%$.

## 7. Recognition Clues & Keywords
- **Keywords:** "Equated to," "Same as," "Equal to."
- **Application:** Apply this when no monetary value (like dollars or rupees) is provided, only quantities.
- **Avoid Confusion:** Do not confuse this with "False Weight" problems (Pattern XIII-XV), which involve physical measurement errors rather than simple quantity-price equivalence.

## 8. Common Mistakes
1. **Denominator Error:** Using $x$ (the C.P. quantity) as the denominator instead of $y$ (the S.P. quantity). Always divide by the quantity associated with the Selling Price.
2. **Sign Confusion:** Forgetting that if the result is negative, it represents a loss.
3. **Ignoring the Unit:** Trying to calculate profit on the total quantity rather than the unit price. Always reduce to a "per article" basis.

## 9. Practice Tips
- **Mastery:** Practice by creating your own scenarios where $x$ and $y$ are multiples of each other to simplify mental math.
- **Related Patterns:** Study "False Weight" problems next, as they also deal with quantity discrepancies but include a "True Value" component.
- **Verification:** Always check your answer: if you sell fewer items for the price of more, your percentage must be positive (profit).
