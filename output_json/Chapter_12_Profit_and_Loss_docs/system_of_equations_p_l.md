# System of Equations: P&L

## Overview
These questions involve two items with distinct cost prices and individual profit/loss percentages that result in a known total profit, loss, or selling price. The core idea is to translate the verbal conditions into a system of two linear equations with two variables ($x$ and $y$) and solve for the unknowns.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "Purchases two items," "Total cost," "Total profit/loss," "Sells at different percentages."
* **Given:** Total cost price (CP) of two items and their individual profit/loss percentages.
* **Asked:** Individual CP of each item or the difference between their prices.
* **Structure:** Two scenarios provided (e.g., two different selling prices or two different profit/loss outcomes).

---

## Important Formulas

### [Linear Profit/Loss Equation]
$$
(\text{Profit/Loss } \%_1 \text{ of } x) \pm (\text{Profit/Loss } \%_2 \text{ of } y) = \text{Total Profit/Loss}
$$
*Meaning:* $x$ and $y$ are the CPs of the two items; percentages are expressed as decimals (e.g., $10\% = 0.10$).
*Use when:* You are given the total profit or loss amount in currency.

### [Equal Selling Price Equation]
$$
(100 \pm P_1)\% \text{ of } x = (100 \pm P_2)\% \text{ of } y
$$
*Meaning:* $P_1$ and $P_2$ are profit/loss percentages; the equation equates the two resulting SPs.
*Use when:* The problem states the items were sold for the "same selling price."

---

## Solution Framework
1. Assign variables $x$ and $y$ to the two unknown Cost Prices.
2. Form the first equation using the total cost: $x + y = \text{Total CP}$.
3. Form the second equation using the profit/loss conditions provided.
4. Solve the system using substitution or elimination.
5. If asked for a difference or specific value, extract it from the solved $x$ and $y$.

---

## Shortcut Tricks
* **Ratio Method for Total CP:** If total profit/loss is zero, the ratio of CPs is inversely proportional to the ratio of their profit/loss percentages ($CP_1 : CP_2 = \text{Loss } \% : \text{Profit } \%$).
* **Why it works:** If there is no net gain/loss, the profit on one must exactly equal the loss on the other ($P_1 \cdot x = L_2 \cdot y$).

---

## Common Mistakes
* **Mixing up SP and Profit:** Using the profit percentage directly in the equation instead of $(100 \pm \%) \cdot CP$.
    * *Fix:* Always convert profit/loss to the final SP multiplier (e.g., $120\%$ for $20\%$ profit).
* **Sign Errors:** Treating a loss as a positive value in the equation.
    * *Fix:* Always subtract loss percentages from $100$ (e.g., $25\%$ loss becomes $75\%$).
* **Variable Confusion:** Solving for $x$ but forgetting to calculate $y$ or the difference between them.
    * *Fix:* Re-read the final question requirement before finalizing the answer.

---

## Similar Patterns
This pattern is distinct from "False Weight" problems because it focuses on algebraic systems of two variables rather than ratio-based weight adjustments.

---

## Revision Summary
**Key formula:** $(100 \pm P_1)\% \cdot x \pm (100 \pm P_2)\% \cdot y = \text{Total Value}$.
**Spot it by:** Two items, two conditions, and two unknown cost prices.
**Fastest method:** Use the ratio of percentages when the net profit/loss is zero.
**Biggest trap:** Forgetting to subtract loss percentages from $100$ when calculating SP.
