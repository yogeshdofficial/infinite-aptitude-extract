# Relative Price Calculation

## Overview

These questions involve items bought or sold at a fraction or percentage of an "original" or "reference" price. The central idea is to assign a base value (usually 100 or $x$) to the reference price and express all subsequent transactions as percentages or fractions of that base.

---

## Recognition Clues

*   **Keywords:** "$\frac{n}{m}$ of its selling price," "purchased at $x\%$ less/more than its value," "sold at $y\%$ profit on the price he had paid."
*   **Given:** A relationship between the transaction price and an unknown original/reference price.
*   **Asked:** Gain/Loss percentage relative to the original price or the cost price.
*   **Structure:** Two-step transaction chain (e.g., Buy at discount $\rightarrow$ Sell at profit on that discounted price).

---

## Important Formulas

### [Relative Cost Price]
$$
\text{C.P.} = (\text{Fraction or \%}) \times \text{Reference Price}
$$
*Meaning:* The actual cost price is derived from the given ratio or percentage of the original/selling price.
*Use when:* The problem states the item was bought at a fraction or percentage of its list/selling price.

### [Relative Selling Price]
$$
\text{S.P.} = (1 \pm \frac{\text{Profit/Loss \%}}{100}) \times \text{C.P.}
$$
*Meaning:* The final selling price is calculated based on the profit or loss applied to the *actual* cost price.
*Use when:* You need to find the final selling price after a percentage gain or loss on the calculated C.P.

---

## Solution Framework

1.  **Assume a Base:** Assign the original price (or S.P.) a value of $100$ (or $x$).
2.  **Calculate C.P.:** Apply the given fraction or percentage discount to the base to find the actual C.P.
3.  **Calculate S.P.:** Apply the profit/loss percentage to the C.P. found in Step 2.
4.  **Find Gain/Loss:** Subtract the original base from the final S.P.
5.  **Calculate Percentage:** Divide the result by the original base (or C.P. as requested) and multiply by $100$.

---

## Shortcut Tricks

*   **The 100-Base Method:** Always assume the original price is $100$. This eliminates the need for $x$ and allows for direct subtraction at the end.
*   **Successive Percentage Logic:** If buying at $20\%$ discount and selling at $40\%$ profit on that price, calculate as $80 \times 1.40 = 112$. The profit on original is $112 - 100 = 12\%$. This is valid because it treats the transaction as a chain of multipliers.

---

## Common Mistakes

*   **Confusing the Base:** Calculating profit percentage on the original price when the question asks for profit on the *actual* cost price.
    *   *Reason:* Misreading the "on the price he had paid" vs "on the original price" clause.
    *   *Fix:* Always re-read the final question sentence to identify the denominator.
*   **Double Discounting:** Applying a profit percentage to the original price instead of the discounted C.P.
    *   *Reason:* Assuming profit is always on the original list price.
    *   *Fix:* Always calculate the actual C.P. first, then apply the profit percentage to that specific value.

---

## Similar Patterns

*   **False Weight Problems:** These are distinct because they involve a ratio of weights rather than a ratio of prices.
*   **Successive Discount Problems:** These are distinct because they involve multiple reductions on the *same* base, whereas this pattern involves a transaction chain (C.P. $\rightarrow$ S.P.).

---

## Revision Summary

**Key formula:** $\text{S.P.} = \text{C.P.} \times (1 + \text{Profit}\%)$.

**Spot it by:** Phrases like "$\frac{n}{m}$ of its price" or "bought at $x\%$ less than value."

**Fastest method:** Assume $100$ as the original price and perform direct arithmetic.

**Biggest trap:** Applying profit percentages to the original price instead of the calculated C.P.
