# Price Change Scenarios

## Overview
These questions involve a two-stage process: an initial transaction (or state) and a hypothetical second transaction. The central idea is to use the first scenario to find the **Cost Price (C.P.)**, which acts as the bridge to calculate the new Selling Price (S.P.) or profit percentage in the second scenario.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "If sold for...", "If the cost is increased by...", "What should be the new selling price...", "What would have been the percentage...".
* **Given:** Original S.P. and profit/loss percentage; or a relationship between C.P. and S.P.
* **Asked:** A new S.P. to achieve a different profit, or a new profit percentage after a price change.

---

## Key Formulas

### [Cost Price from S.P.]
$$
\text{C.P.} = \text{S.P.} \times \frac{100}{100 \pm \text{Profit/Loss}\%}
$$
**Variables:** S.P. = Selling Price, Profit% = Gain, Loss% = Loss.
**When to use:** When you have the S.P. and need the base C.P. to start the calculation.
**Worked example:** Q6: S.P. = 1950, Loss = 25%. $\text{C.P.} = 1950 \times \frac{100}{75} = 2600$.

### [New S.P. from C.P.]
$$
\text{New S.P.} = \text{C.P.} \times \frac{100 \pm \text{New Profit/Loss}\%}{100}
$$
**Variables:** C.P. = Cost Price, New Profit/Loss% = Target percentage.
**When to use:** When you have the C.P. and need to find the price for a specific target profit.
**Worked example:** Q6: C.P. = 2600, Profit = 30%. $\text{New S.P.} = 2600 \times \frac{130}{100} = 3380$.

---

## Solution Framework

1. **Step 1: Find the C.P.** — Use the initial S.P. and profit/loss % to calculate the base cost.
2. **Step 2: Adjust for changes (if any)** — If the question mentions a change in production cost, apply that % increase/decrease to the C.P. found in Step 1.
3. **Step 3: Calculate the target** — Apply the new profit/loss % to the (adjusted) C.P. to find the final S.P. or profit %.

---

## Shortcut Tricks

**Trick:** Use the ratio method for direct S.P. changes: $\frac{S.P._1}{100 \pm P_1\%} = \frac{S.P._2}{100 \pm P_2\%}$.
**Why it works:** Since C.P. is constant, the ratio of S.P. to its percentage factor is constant.
**When to use:** When there is no change in the actual production cost (C.P.).
**Example:** Q6: $\frac{1950}{75} = \frac{x}{130} \implies x = \frac{1950 \times 130}{75} = 3380$.

---

## Common Mistakes
* **Mistake:** Adding/subtracting percentages (e.g., $20\% + 15\%$). **Fix:** Always calculate the new value sequentially (e.g., $1.15 \times \text{Old C.P.}$).
* **Mistake:** Calculating profit % on the S.P. **Fix:** Always divide the profit amount by the C.P.
* **Mistake:** Applying the new profit % to the old S.P. **Fix:** Always apply profit % to the C.P.

---

## Worked Example (Step-by-Step)

**Question:** A television manufacturer earns 20% profit by selling each T.V. set for ₹14400. If the production cost is increased by 15%, what should be the new selling price to gain 15%?

**Solution:**
1. **Find C.P.:** $\text{C.P.} = 14400 \times \frac{100}{120} = 12000$.
2. **Adjust C.P.:** New C.P. = $12000 \times 1.15 = 13800$.
3. **Calculate New S.P.:** $\text{New S.P.} = 13800 \times 1.15 = 15870$.

**Answer:** ₹15870.

## Similar Patterns
**False Weight Patterns:** Distinguished by the mention of "false weights" or "less than actual weight" instead of simple price changes.

---

## Revision Summary
* **Key formula:** $\text{S.P.} = \text{C.P.} \times (1 \pm \text{Profit/Loss}\%)$.
* **Spot it by:** "If sold for..." or "If cost increases..." phrases.
* **First move:** Calculate the original C.P. immediately.
* **Fastest method:** Use the ratio method if C.P. remains constant.
* **Biggest trap:** Applying profit % to the wrong base (S.P. instead of C.P.).

---

## Appendix: Prerequisites

### Concepts You Must Know
* **Percentage Multipliers:** A $20\%$ profit means multiplying C.P. by $1.20$; a $20\%$ loss means multiplying by $0.80$.
* **Fractional Parts:** $\frac{9}{10}$ of a value means $0.9 \times \text{Value}$.

### Formulas You Must Know First
$$
\text{Profit} = \text{S.P.} - \text{C.P.}
$$
**What it means:** Profit is the surplus after covering costs.
**Example:** If S.P. is 120 and C.P. is 100, Profit = 20.

### Terms Used In This Pattern
* **C.P. (Cost Price):** The amount spent to acquire or produce an item.
* **S.P. (Selling Price):** The amount received after selling an item.
