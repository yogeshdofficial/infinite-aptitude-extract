# Multiple Item Transactions

## Overview
These questions involve calculating profit or loss when dealing with bulk quantities, overhead expenses, or partial sales. The central idea is to normalize all values to a **Total Cost Price (Total C.P.)** and **Total Selling Price (Total S.P.)** before calculating the percentage.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "Articles," "dozens," "reams," "kg," "overhead expenses," "transportation," "unsold/broken items."
* **Given:** Multiple units, varying purchase/sale rates, or additional costs (repairs/transport).
* **Asked:** Overall profit/loss percentage or the required selling price to achieve a target profit.
* **Scan for:** "Total outlay," "overall profit," or "sold at the rate of."

---

## Important Formulas

### Total Profit/Loss Percentage
$$
\text{Profit \%} = \left( \frac{\text{Total S.P.} - \text{Total C.P.}}{\text{Total C.P.}} \right) \times 100
$$
*Meaning:* The ratio of net gain to the total investment.
*Use when:* You have calculated the aggregate cost and aggregate revenue.

### Total Cost Price (with Overheads)
$$
\text{Total C.P.} = (\text{Quantity} \times \text{Rate}) + \text{Overheads}
$$
*Meaning:* Sum of purchase price and all additional expenses (repairs, transport, octroi).
*Use when:* The problem mentions extra costs incurred before the sale.

---

## Solution Framework
1. **Normalize:** Calculate the total quantity of items available after accounting for broken or useless units.
2. **Aggregate Cost:** Sum the purchase price of all items plus all overhead expenses (repairs, transport, etc.).
3. **Aggregate Revenue:** Calculate the total money received by multiplying the number of items sold by their respective selling prices.
4. **Net Result:** Subtract Total C.P. from Total S.P. to find the absolute profit or loss.
5. **Percentage:** Divide the result by Total C.P. and multiply by 100.

---

## Shortcut Tricks
* **Unitary Method:** If quantities are large, calculate the C.P. and S.P. for a single unit (e.g., per kg or per pen) to simplify the percentage calculation.
* **Ratio Scaling:** If you need to find the S.P. for a target profit, use: $\text{Total S.P.} = \text{Total C.P.} \times (1 + \frac{\text{Profit \%}}{100})$.
* **Validity:** These are valid because profit percentage is independent of the absolute number of items, provided the cost and revenue are scaled proportionally.

---

## Common Mistakes
* **Ignoring Broken Items:** Calculating S.P. based on the initial quantity instead of the remaining quantity. *Fix:* Always subtract "broken" or "useless" items from the total count before calculating S.P.
* **Forgetting Overheads:** Calculating profit only on the purchase price. *Fix:* Add all expenses (transport, repairs, coolie charges) to the C.P. before calculating profit.
* **Mixing Units:** Using "per dozen" price for some items and "per piece" for others. *Fix:* Convert everything to a single unit (e.g., all to "per piece") before summing.

---

## Similar Patterns
* **False Weight Patterns:** These are distinct because they involve a "dishonest" ratio rather than simple bulk arithmetic.
* **Successive Transactions:** These involve a chain of buyers (A to B to C), whereas Multiple Item Transactions focus on a single trader managing a bulk inventory.

---

## Revision Summary
**Key formula:** $\text{Profit \%} = \frac{\text{Total S.P.} - \text{Total C.P.}}{\text{Total C.P.}} \times 100$.
**Spot it by:** Multiple units, overhead costs, or broken/unsold inventory.
**Fastest method:** Calculate Total C.P. and Total S.P. first, then find the percentage.
**Biggest trap:** Forgetting to subtract broken items from the total quantity sold.
