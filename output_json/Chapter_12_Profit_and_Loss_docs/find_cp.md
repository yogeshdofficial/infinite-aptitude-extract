# Find CP (Cost Price)

## Overview
These questions provide a final transaction value (Selling Price) and a percentage change (Profit/Loss/Tax) to find the original investment. The central idea is that the Selling Price represents a specific percentage of the Cost Price, allowing you to reverse the calculation.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "Sold for", "Paid", "Purchased for", "Including sales tax", "Gain of", "Loss of".
* **Given:** A final amount (SP) and a percentage value (Profit%, Loss%, or Tax%).
* **Asked:** "What was the cost price?" or "What was the original price?"
* **Scan:** Look for the phrase "sold for [Amount] at a [Profit/Loss] of [X]%".

---

## Important Formulas

### Basic Cost Price Calculation
$$
\text{C.P.} = \text{S.P.} \times \left( \frac{100}{100 \pm \text{Percentage}} \right)
$$
*Meaning:* Use $+ \text{Profit}\%$ or $-\text{Loss}\%$.
*Use when:* You have a single profit or loss percentage relative to the CP.

### Tax-Inclusive Cost Price
$$
\text{C.P.} = \frac{\text{Total Paid}}{(1 + \text{Tax Rate}) \times (1 + \text{Profit Rate})}
$$
*Meaning:* Tax is applied to the SP, while profit is applied to the CP.
*Use when:* The question mentions both sales tax and profit percentage on the same item.

---

## Solution Framework
1. Identify the final amount (SP) and the percentage change.
2. Determine if the percentage is a Profit (+) or a Loss (-).
3. Set up the fraction: $\frac{100}{100 \pm \%}$.
4. Multiply the SP by this fraction.
5. If tax is involved, divide the total amount by $(1 + \text{Tax Rate})$ first to isolate the base SP, then apply the profit formula.

---

## Shortcut Tricks
* **Fractional Multipliers:** Convert percentages to fractions (e.g., $20\% = \frac{1}{5}$). If profit is $20\%$, CP is $\frac{5}{6}$ of SP.
* **Mental Check:** If there is a profit, the CP must be smaller than the SP. If there is a loss, the CP must be larger than the SP. Use this to eliminate impossible options immediately.

---

## Common Mistakes
* **Applying Tax to CP:** Adding tax to the CP instead of the SP. *Fix:* Tax is always calculated on the price the customer pays (SP).
* **Mixing up the denominator:** Using $100 \pm \%$ as the numerator. *Fix:* Remember that CP is the base ($100\%$), so $100$ must be the numerator.
* **Ignoring "List Price":** Confusing List Price with CP when tax is involved. *Fix:* List Price is the base before tax; calculate it by dividing the tax-inclusive price by $(1 + \text{Tax Rate})$.

---

## Similar Patterns
* **Profit/Loss % Calculation:** These ask for the *percentage* (result), whereas this pattern asks for the *original value* (input).
* **False Weight Patterns:** These involve a change in quantity, not just price; look for "grams" or "kg" keywords to distinguish them.

---

## Revision Summary
**Key formula:** $\text{C.P.} = \text{S.P.} \times \frac{100}{100 \pm \%}$.
**Spot it by:** Looking for a final price and a percentage change.
**Fastest method:** Use fractional multipliers to avoid large division.
**Biggest trap:** Applying tax percentages to the CP instead of the SP.
