# Two-Stage Price Change

## Overview

These problems involve an item being sold at a specific price with a known profit or loss, followed by a requirement to find a new selling price for a different profit/loss target. The central idea is to use the Cost Price (C.P.) as the bridge between the two scenarios.

---

## Recognition Clues

*   **Keywords:** "Sold at... [profit/loss]%", "At what price must it be sold to gain/lose...%", "If the price were...".
*   **Given:** One S.P. and its corresponding profit/loss percentage.
*   **Asked:** A new S.P. for a different target profit/loss percentage.
*   **Structure:** Two distinct scenarios linked by a constant C.P.

---

## Important Formulas

### [Cost Price from S.P.]

$$
\text{C.P.} = \text{S.P.} \times \left( \frac{100}{100 \pm \text{Profit/Loss}\%} \right)
$$

*Meaning:* Converts a known S.P. back to the original investment.
*Use when:* You need the base value to calculate the second S.P.

### [Proportionality Method]

$$
\frac{\text{S.P.}_1}{100 \pm \text{P/L}_1\%} = \frac{\text{S.P.}_2}{100 \pm \text{P/L}_2\%}
$$

*Meaning:* The ratio of S.P. to the percentage factor is constant.
*Use when:* You want to skip calculating the actual C.P. and solve in one step.

---

## Solution Framework

1.  Identify the first S.P. and its associated percentage (e.g., $100 + \text{Profit}\%$ or $100 - \text{Loss}\%$).
2.  Calculate the C.P. using the formula or set up the ratio $\frac{\text{S.P.}_1}{\text{Factor}_1} = \frac{\text{S.P.}_2}{\text{Factor}_2}$.
3.  If calculating C.P. first, multiply it by the new target percentage factor to find the new S.P.
4.  If using the ratio method, cross-multiply to solve for the unknown S.P. directly.

---

## Shortcut Tricks

*   **The Ratio Shortcut:** Use $\text{S.P.}_2 = \text{S.P.}_1 \times \frac{\text{New Factor}}{\text{Old Factor}}$.
*   **Why:** It eliminates the need to find the intermediate C.P., saving time and reducing calculation errors.

---

## Common Mistakes

*   **Adding/Subtracting from S.P.:** Adding profit directly to S.P. instead of C.P.
    *   *Reason:* Profit/Loss is always calculated on C.P., not S.P.
    *   *Fix:* Always find C.P. or use the ratio factor first.
*   **Mixing up Profit and Loss signs:** Using $100 + \text{Loss}\%$ instead of $100 - \text{Loss}\%$.
    *   *Reason:* Careless reading of the question.
    *   *Fix:* Circle the word "loss" or "profit" in the question paper immediately.
*   **Ignoring the "New C.P." change:** In questions like Q7, the C.P. itself changes.
    *   *Reason:* Assuming C.P. is constant in every two-stage problem.
    *   *Fix:* Read carefully if the "production cost" or "investment" is modified.

---

## Similar Patterns

*   **False Weight Problems:** These also involve two stages but use a different formula (Error/True Value).
*   **Successive Percentage Change:** Often confused with this, but involves changing the *same* item's price twice, whereas this pattern usually involves finding a *target* price.

---

## Revision Summary

**Key formula:** $\text{S.P.}_2 = \text{S.P.}_1 \times \frac{\text{New Factor}}{\text{Old Factor}}$

**Spot it by:** Two S.P. scenarios with different profit/loss percentages.

**Fastest method:** Use the ratio of factors to avoid calculating C.P.

**Biggest trap:** Forgetting that profit/loss is always a percentage of C.P., not S.P.
