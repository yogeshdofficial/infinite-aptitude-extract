# Problems On Ages — Chapter Overview

## What This Chapter Is About

This chapter deals with the mathematical relationships between the ages of individuals at different points in time. The central quantity is the "age gap," which remains constant regardless of whether you are looking at the past, present, or future. Problems typically involve using ratios, algebraic equations, or averages to determine the current age of one or more people based on clues about their relative ages at various time intervals.

---

## Core Concepts

**[The Constant Age Difference]** — The difference between the ages of two people is a permanent value that never changes over time. If a father is 20 years older than his son today, he will still be 20 years older than his son 10, 20, or 50 years from now.

**[Time-Shifting]** — Every age calculation must be anchored to a specific point in time, usually the "present." To move from the present to the past, you subtract years; to move to the future, you add years. For example, if someone is $x$ years old today, their age 5 years ago was $(x - 5)$ and their age 5 years hence will be $(x + 5)$.

**[Ratio Scaling]** — Ratios represent the relative size of ages, not the absolute values. If the ratio of two people's ages is $3:4$, their actual ages are $3x$ and $4x$. The variable $x$ acts as a common multiplier that links the ratio to real-world years.

**[Algebraic Translation]** — Most problems are essentially word puzzles that must be translated into linear equations. You must identify the "unknown" (usually the present age) and express all other conditions (past or future ages) in terms of that single variable.

---

## Key Terms Glossary

**Present Age** — The age of a person at the current moment, usually represented by a variable like $x$.

**Hence / After / Future** — Terms indicating a time in the future. If a person is $x$ years old now, their age "after $n$ years" is $(x + n)$.

**Ago / Before / Past** — Terms indicating a time in the past. If a person is $x$ years old now, their age "$n$ years ago" was $(x - n)$.

**Ratio** — A comparison of two quantities by division, written as $a:b$. It indicates that for every $a$ units of one person's age, there are $b$ units of another's.

**Difference** — The result of subtracting the smaller age from the larger age. Because the age gap is constant, this value is a powerful tool for solving equations.

## Core Formulas

### The Age-Difference Equation
$$
(A_1 \pm t) - (B_1 \pm t) = A_1 - B_1
$$
**Variables:**
- $A_1, B_1$ = Present ages of person A and person B.
- $t$ = Number of years passed or elapsed.

**When to use:** When you need to relate two people's ages across different time periods.

**Key insight:** The time factor $t$ cancels out when calculating the difference, proving the gap is constant.

**Worked example:** If A is 30 and B is 14, the difference is 16. After 6 years, A is 36 and B is 20; the difference remains $36 - 20 = 16$.

## Chapter-Wide Traps

**The "Time-Shift" Error:** Forgetting to add or subtract the time interval from *both* people in a ratio. → Always apply the time change to every individual mentioned in the problem.

**Ratio Misinterpretation:** Treating a ratio like $3:4$ as actual years (3 years and 4 years) instead of $3x$ and $4x$. → Always introduce a constant multiplier ($x$) when working with ratios.

**Ignoring the "Constant Difference":** Failing to use the fact that the age gap between two people never changes. → Use the constant difference as a shortcut to verify your answers or simplify complex equations.
