# Fraction Problems

## Overview
These problems involve finding an unknown fraction based on conditions applied to its numerator and denominator. The central idea is to represent the fraction as $\frac{x}{y}$ and translate the word-problem conditions into a system of linear equations.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "fraction becomes", "numerator", "denominator", "added to", "subtracted from", "product of two fractions".
* **Given:** Relationships between numerator and denominator (e.g., "3 more than"), or results of operations (e.g., "becomes $\frac{2}{3}$").
* **Asked:** The original fraction or the value of one of the fractions.

---

## Key Formulas

### [Fraction Representation]
$$
\text{Fraction} = \frac{x}{y}
$$
**Variables:** $x$ = numerator, $y$ = denominator.
**When to use:** Every problem where a single fraction is described.
**Worked example:** If a fraction becomes $\frac{2}{3}$ when 1 is added to both, set $\frac{x+1}{y+1} = \frac{2}{3}$.

### [Product/Quotient Relationship]
$$
\text{Product} = ab, \quad \text{Quotient} = \frac{a}{b} \implies b^2 = \frac{\text{Product}}{\text{Quotient}}
$$
**Variables:** $a, b$ = two unknown fractions.
**When to use:** When given the product and quotient of two distinct fractions.
**Worked example:** Product $\frac{14}{15}$, Quotient $\frac{35}{24}$. $b^2 = \frac{14}{15} \div \frac{35}{24} = \frac{14}{15} \times \frac{24}{35} = \frac{16}{25} \implies b = \frac{4}{5}$.

---

## Solution Framework
1. **Define variables:** Assign $x$ and $y$ to the numerator and denominator (or $a$ and $b$ for two fractions).
2. **Translate conditions:** Convert each sentence into a mathematical equation.
3. **Simplify:** Cross-multiply to remove fractions and bring variables to one side.
4. **Solve system:** Use substitution or elimination to find the values of the variables.
5. **State final fraction:** Write the result in the form $\frac{x}{y}$.

---

## Shortcut Tricks
* **Trick:** For product/quotient problems, always divide the product by the quotient to isolate the square of one variable.
* **Why it works:** $\frac{ab}{a/b} = ab \times \frac{b}{a} = b^2$.
* **When to use:** When you have two fractions $a$ and $b$ and their product and quotient are provided.
* **Example:** $ab = \frac{14}{15}, \frac{a}{b} = \frac{35}{24} \implies b^2 = \frac{16}{25} \implies b = \frac{4}{5}$.

---

## Common Mistakes
* **Mistake:** Adding/subtracting values to the fraction as a whole instead of specifically to the numerator or denominator.
* **Why it happens:** Misreading "1 is added to both its numerator and denominator."
* **Fix:** Always write $\frac{x+1}{y+1}$ explicitly.
* **Mistake:** Forgetting to take the square root when solving for $b^2$ or $A^2$.
* **Why it happens:** Rushing the final calculation.
* **Fix:** Always check if your result is a square before finalizing.

---

## Worked Example (Step-by-Step)

**Question:** The denominator of a fraction is 3 more than the numerator. If the numerator as well as the denominator is increased by 4, the fraction becomes $\frac{4}{5}$. What was the original fraction?

**Solution:**
1. **Define:** Let numerator $= x$, then denominator $= x + 3$. Fraction is $\frac{x}{x+3}$.
2. **Translate:** $\frac{x+4}{(x+3)+4} = \frac{4}{5}$.
3. **Simplify:** $\frac{x+4}{x+7} = \frac{4}{5} \implies 5(x+4) = 4(x+7)$.
4. **Solve:** $5x + 20 = 4x + 28 \implies x = 8$.
5. **Finalize:** Numerator $= 8$, Denominator $= 8+3 = 11$. Fraction is $\frac{8}{11}$.

**Answer:** $\frac{8}{11}$

## Similar Patterns
**Problems on Numbers:** These involve general numbers rather than fractions; distinguish by looking for the specific mention of "numerator" and "denominator."

---

## Revision Summary
* **Key formula:** $\frac{x}{y}$ for single fractions; $b^2 = \frac{\text{Product}}{\text{Quotient}}$ for two fractions.
* **Spot it by:** Looking for "numerator", "denominator", or "fraction becomes".
* **First move:** Assign variables $x$ and $y$ and write the fraction as $\frac{x}{y}$.
* **Fastest method:** Cross-multiply immediately to clear the fraction.
* **Biggest trap:** Adding constants to the whole fraction instead of just the numerator/denominator.

---

## Appendix: Prerequisites

### Concepts You Must Know
* **Linear Equations:** Ability to solve $ax + by = c$ using substitution or elimination.
* **Cross-Multiplication:** The rule $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d} \implies ad = bc$.

### Formulas You Must Know First
* **Substitution:** If $x+y=11$, then $y=11-x$.

### Terms Used In This Pattern
* **Numerator:** The top part of a fraction.
* **Denominator:** The bottom part of a fraction.
* **Reciprocal:** The inverse of a fraction (e.g., $\frac{x}{y}$ becomes $\frac{y}{x}$).
