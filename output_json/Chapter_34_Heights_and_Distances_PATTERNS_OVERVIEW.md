# Heights And Distances — Chapter Overview

## What This Chapter Is About

This chapter deals with the application of trigonometry to calculate the heights of tall objects or the distances between points that are otherwise difficult to measure directly. The central relationship in every problem is the ratio between the sides of a right-angled triangle, where the angle of observation connects the horizontal distance to the vertical height. You will encounter two primary scenarios: calculating a single unknown dimension from one observation point, or determining dimensions by analyzing how the angle of observation changes as you move between two different points.

---

## Core Concepts

**[The Right-Angled Triangle Model]** — Every problem in this chapter is essentially a right-angled triangle hidden in a real-world scene. You must identify the "vertical" side (the height), the "horizontal" side (the distance along the ground), and the "line of sight" (the hypotenuse) to apply trigonometric ratios. For example, a ladder leaning against a wall forms the hypotenuse, while the wall and ground form the perpendicular and base.

**[The Horizontal Reference Line]** — Trigonometry requires an angle measured from a horizontal line. Whether you are looking up or down, you must always draw an imaginary horizontal line from the observer's eye level to serve as the baseline for your angle. If a man is on top of a tower, his horizontal line is parallel to the ground at the tower's peak, not the ground itself.

**[Angle of Elevation vs. Depression]** — These are simply two ways of describing the same geometric relationship. Elevation is the angle looking *up* from a horizontal line, while depression is the angle looking *down* from a horizontal line. Because of the properties of parallel lines (alternate interior angles), the angle of depression from the top of an object is always equal to the angle of elevation from the bottom of that object.

**[Constant Ratios]** — The core of the math lies in the fact that for a fixed angle, the ratio of the sides of a triangle remains constant regardless of the triangle's size. This allows us to use the tangent ($\tan \theta$) function to link height and distance, as it directly relates the "opposite" side (height) to the "adjacent" side (distance).

---

## Key Terms Glossary

**Line of Sight** — The imaginary straight line drawn from the observer's eye to the object being viewed.

**Angle of Elevation** — The angle formed between the horizontal line and the line of sight when looking *up* at an object.

**Angle of Depression** — The angle formed between the horizontal line and the line of sight when looking *down* at an object.

**Horizontal Line** — A line parallel to the ground passing through the observer's eye level; it acts as the reference point for all angles.

## Core Formulas

### Trigonometric Ratios (SOH-CAH-TOA)

$$
\sin \theta = \frac{\text{Perpendicular}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}, \quad \cos \theta = \frac{\text{Base}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}, \quad \tan \theta = \frac{\text{Perpendicular}}{\text{Base}}
$$

**Variables:**
- $\theta$ = The angle of elevation or depression.
- $\text{Perpendicular}$ = The vertical height of the object.
- $\text{Base}$ = The horizontal distance from the observer to the object.
- $\text{Hypotenuse}$ = The direct line of sight distance.

**When to use:** Whenever you need to relate two sides of a right triangle given one angle.

**Key insight:** $\tan \theta$ is the most frequently used formula because most problems involve height and ground distance, ignoring the hypotenuse.

**Worked example:** If a pole is $10\text{ m}$ high and the angle of elevation is $45^\circ$, then $\tan 45^\circ = \frac{10}{\text{Base}} \implies 1 = \frac{10}{\text{Base}} \implies \text{Base} = 10\text{ m}$.

## Chapter-Wide Traps

**[Ignoring the Observer's Height]:** If a problem mentions a person of a certain height observing an object, the "height" of the triangle starts from their eye level, not the ground → Always subtract the observer's height from the total height of the object if necessary.

**[Mixing up Degrees and Radians]:** Using the wrong value for $\tan 30^\circ$ or $\tan 60^\circ$ is a common calculation error → Memorize the standard values ($30^\circ, 45^\circ, 60^\circ$) perfectly before starting.

**[Confusing Base and Hypotenuse]:** Students often use $\sin$ when they need $\tan$ because they misidentify the "distance along the ground" as the "line of sight" → Always sketch the triangle and label the sides before picking a formula.
