# Number System — Chapter Overview

## What This Chapter Is About

This chapter deals with the fundamental properties, classification, and behavior of numbers within the Hindu-Arabic decimal system. It focuses on the relationships between digits, the rules governing divisibility, and the algebraic structures that allow us to manipulate large or complex numerical expressions efficiently. Every problem in this chapter revolves around identifying the "DNA" of a number—its factors, its remainder when divided, or its position within a sequence—to solve for an unknown value.

---

## Core Concepts

**[Place Value and Face Value]** — The face value is the digit's inherent worth, while the place value is its worth based on its position (ones, tens, hundreds, etc.). For example, in $528$, the face value of $5$ is $5$, but its place value is $500$ because it occupies the hundreds column.

**[Number Classification]** — Numbers are categorized by their properties: Natural numbers are for counting ($1, 2, 3...$), Whole numbers include zero, and Integers include negatives. Understanding these sets is vital because a rule that applies to all integers might fail if you only consider natural numbers.

**[Prime vs. Composite Numbers]** — A prime number has exactly two factors ($1$ and itself), while a composite number has more than two. Knowing that $2$ is the only even prime number is a critical mental shortcut for solving divisibility and factor-based problems.

**[Divisibility Logic]** — This is the study of whether one number can be divided by another without leaving a remainder. By breaking a number down into its prime factors, you can determine its divisibility without performing long division.

**[Remainder Dynamics]** — When a number is not perfectly divisible, the "leftover" is the remainder. The core idea is that the remainder of a large product is the same as the product of the individual remainders of its components.

## Key Terms Glossary

**Numeral** — A symbol or group of symbols used to represent a number (e.g., "43" is a numeral representing the number forty-three).

**Co-primes** — Two numbers are co-prime if their Highest Common Factor (H.C.F.) is $1$. They do not need to be prime themselves; for example, $8$ and $9$ are co-prime because they share no common factors other than $1$.

**Twin Primes** — A pair of prime numbers that have a difference of $2$, such as $(3, 5)$ or $(11, 13)$.

**Perfect Number** — A number where the sum of its proper divisors (excluding the number itself) equals the number. For example, $6$ is perfect because $1 + 2 + 3 = 6$.

**Rational Number** — Any number that can be written as a fraction $\frac{p}{q}$ where $p$ and $q$ are integers and $q \neq 0$.

## Core Formulas

### [Division Algorithm]

$$
\text{Dividend} = (\text{Divisor} \times \text{Quotient}) + \text{Remainder}
$$

**Variables:**
- Dividend: The number being divided.
- Divisor: The number doing the dividing.
- Quotient: The result of the division.
- Remainder: The amount left over.

**When to use:** Use this when you are given three of the four components and need to find the missing one.

**Key insight:** The remainder must always be smaller than the divisor.

**Worked example:** If $17 \div 5$, then $17 = (5 \times 3) + 2$.

## Chapter-Wide Traps

**The "Zero" Trap:** Forgetting that $0$ is a whole number but not a natural number, or assuming division by zero is possible → Always check if your variable could be zero before dividing.

**The "1" Trap:** Treating $1$ as a prime number → Remember that $1$ is neither prime nor composite; it is a unique unit.

**The "Negative" Trap:** Assuming properties of positive integers (like divisibility) apply identically to negative integers → Always verify the sign of the result when dealing with negative numbers.
