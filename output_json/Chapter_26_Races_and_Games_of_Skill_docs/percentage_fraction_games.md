# Percentage Fraction Games

## Overview
These questions ask you to determine the portion of a total (games won or money held) represented by a specific part. The central idea is to express the "part" as a ratio of the "whole" and convert that ratio into a percentage or a simplified fraction.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "What percent of...", "What fraction of...", "won in", "total money".
* **Given:** A total count (e.g., 40 games) and a specific subset (e.g., 24 wins).
* **Asked:** The percentage or fraction representing that subset relative to the total.
* **Relationship clues:** Phrases like "4 times as much as" to define parts of a total.

---

## Key Formulas

### [Percentage Calculation]
$$
\text{Percentage} = \left( \frac{\text{Part}}{\text{Whole}} \right) \times 100
$$
**Variables:**
- $\text{Part}$ = The specific subset (e.g., games won).
- $\text{Whole}$ = The total set (e.g., total games played).

**When to use:** When the question asks for a percentage of the total.

**Worked example:** Games won = 24, Total games = 40. $\frac{24}{40} \times 100 = 0.6 \times 100 = 60\%$.

### [Fraction Simplification]
$$
\text{Simplified Fraction} = \frac{\text{Numerator} \div \text{GCD}}{\text{Denominator} \div \text{GCD}}
$$
**Variables:**
- $\text{GCD}$ = Greatest Common Divisor of the numerator and denominator.

**When to use:** When expressing a part as a fraction in its simplest form.

**Worked example:** J won 5 out of 60. $\frac{5}{60}$. GCD is 5. $\frac{5 \div 5}{60 \div 5} = \frac{1}{12}$.

---

## Solution Framework
1. **Identify the Whole:** Determine the total number of units (games or total money).
2. **Identify the Part:** Determine the specific number of units being asked about.
3. **Form the Ratio:** Write the relationship as $\frac{\text{Part}}{\text{Whole}}$.
4. **Convert/Simplify:** Multiply by 100 for percentage or divide both terms by the GCD for a fraction.

---

## Shortcut Tricks
* **Trick:** Use "Total Parts" logic for money distribution.
* **Why it works:** If $J+B = 4T$, then $T + (J+B) = 5T = \text{Total}$.
* **When to use:** When the question gives a relationship between players' shares rather than absolute values.
* **Example:** If $J+B = 4T$, then $T$ is $\frac{1}{1+4} = \frac{1}{5}$ of the total.

---

## Common Mistakes
* **Mistake:** Dividing the total by the part (e.g., $\frac{40}{24}$).
    * **Why it happens:** Rushing and misreading the "Part of Whole" relationship.
    * **Fix:** Always check if the result is $< 1$ (for fractions) or $< 100$ (for percentages).
* **Mistake:** Confusing "4 times as much" with "4 times more".
    * **Why it happens:** Linguistic ambiguity.
    * **Fix:** "4 times as much" means $4 \times$ the value; "4 times more" usually means $5 \times$ the value.

---

## Worked Example (Step-by-Step)

**Question:** A team played 40 games in a season and won in 24 of them. What percent of games played did the team win?

**Solution:**
1. **Identify the Whole:** Total games = 40.
2. **Identify the Part:** Games won = 24.
3. **Form the Ratio:** $\frac{24}{40}$.
4. **Convert:** $\frac{24}{40} \times 100 = \frac{3}{5} \times 100 = 60\%$.

**Answer:** 60%

## Similar Patterns
* **Ratio/Proportion Problems:** These involve comparing two different entities, whereas this pattern focuses on a part-to-whole relationship.

---

## Revision Summary
* **Key formula:** $\frac{\text{Part}}{\text{Whole}} \times 100$ (for %) or $\frac{\text{Part}}{\text{Whole}}$ (for fraction).
* **Spot it by:** Looking for "What percent/fraction of total".
* **First move:** Clearly define the "Whole" and the "Part".
* **Fastest method:** Simplify the fraction before multiplying by 100.
* **Biggest trap:** Dividing the total by the part instead of the part by the total.

---

## Appendix: Prerequisites

### Concepts You Must Know
* **Ratio** — A comparison of two quantities by division.
* **GCD (Greatest Common Divisor)** — The largest number that divides two numbers without leaving a remainder.
* **Percentage** — A number expressed as a fraction of 100.

### Formulas You Must Know First
* **Total = Sum of Parts** — The whole is the sum of all individual components.

### Terms Used In This Pattern
* **Part** — The specific subset of the total.
* **Whole** — The complete set or total amount.
