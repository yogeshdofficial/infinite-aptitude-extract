# Compound Proportion

## Overview
This pattern involves problems where multiple variables (men, days, hours, work) change simultaneously. You solve these by identifying whether each variable is directly or inversely proportional to the target variable and using the Chain Rule to equate the two scenarios.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Multiple variables:** The question lists at least three factors (e.g., men, hours, days, work).
* **"At the same rate":** A standard phrase indicating the relationship between variables remains constant.
* **Two scenarios:** The question provides a complete set of data for one case and asks for a missing value in a second case.
* **Target variable:** You are almost always asked to find a missing quantity (days, men, or length) given a change in the other variables.

---

## Key Formulas

### The Chain Rule
$$
\frac{M_1 \times D_1 \times H_1}{W_1} = \frac{M_2 \times D_2 \times H_2}{W_2}
$$

**Variables:**
- $M$ = Men (or workers/compositors)
- $D$ = Days
- $H$ = Hours per day
- $W$ = Work done (pages, length of wall, etc.)

**When to use:** When you have two sets of conditions and need to find one missing variable.

**Worked example:** Using Q5 (5 students, 18 pencils, 9 days):
$\frac{5 \times 9}{18} = \frac{15 \times x}{66} \implies 2.5 = \frac{15x}{66} \implies x = 11$.

---

## Solution Framework

1. **Identify variables:** List all given quantities and assign them to $M, D, H, W$ (or equivalent categories).
2. **Determine proportionality:** For each variable, ask: "If this increases, does the target variable increase (Direct) or decrease (Inverse)?"
3. **Set up the equation:** Place all "Work" terms in the denominator and all other terms in the numerator on both sides.
4. **Substitute values:** Plug in the known numbers, using $x$ for the unknown.
5. **Solve for $x$:** Use cross-multiplication to isolate $x$ and calculate the final result.

---

## Shortcut Tricks

* **Trick:** The "Work" is always the denominator.
* **Why it works:** Work is the only variable that is always directly proportional to the time/men required to complete it.
* **When to use it:** Every time the question mentions "work," "pages," "metres of wall," or "pencils used."
* **Example:** In Q6, length of wall (56m) is work. Put it under the men/days: $\frac{20 \times 6}{56} = \frac{35 \times 3}{x}$.

---

## Common Mistakes

* **Mistake:** Putting "Work" in the numerator.
    * **Why it happens:** Forgetting that more work requires more time/men.
    * **Fix:** Always place the "output" (pages, wall length) in the denominator.
* **Mistake:** Mixing up direct and inverse relationships.
    * **Why it happens:** Guessing instead of testing the logic (e.g., "If I have more men, will it take more or less time?").
    * **Fix:** Always ask: "If I increase this, does the target increase?"
* **Mistake:** Ignoring units (e.g., $10\frac{1}{2}$ hours).
    * **Why it happens:** Rushing the calculation.
    * **Fix:** Convert all mixed fractions to improper fractions (e.g., $10.5 = \frac{21}{2}$) before calculating.

---

## Worked Example (Step-by-Step)

**Question:** 8 men working for 9 hours a day complete a piece of work in 20 days. In how many days can 7 men working for 10 hours a day complete the same piece of work?

**Solution:**
1. **Identify:** $M_1=8, H_1=9, D_1=20$; $M_2=7, H_2=10, D_2=x$. Work is the same, so $W_1=W_2=1$.
2. **Proportionality:** Men and Days are inverse; Hours and Days are inverse.
3. **Equation:** $M_1 \times H_1 \times D_1 = M_2 \times H_2 \times D_2$.
4. **Substitute:** $8 \times 9 \times 20 = 7 \times 10 \times x$.
5. **Solve:** $1440 = 70x \implies x = \frac{1440}{70} = 20\frac{4}{7}$.

**Answer:** $20\frac{4}{7}$ days.

---

## Similar Patterns
**Work-Efficiency Problems:** These are identical to Compound Proportion but often introduce "efficiency" (e.g., A is twice as fast as B). Treat efficiency as an additional multiplier in the $M$ (men) variable.

---

## Revision Summary
* **Key formula:** $\frac{M_1 D_1 H_1}{W_1} = \frac{M_2 D_2 H_2}{W_2}$.
* **Spot it by:** Multiple variables given for two different scenarios.
* **First move:** Identify which variable is the "Work" (denominator).
* **Fastest method:** Use the Chain Rule equation directly.
* **Biggest trap:** Putting the "Work" in the numerator.

---

## Appendix: Prerequisites

### Concepts You Must Know
* **Direct Proportion** — As one value increases, the other increases at the same rate.
* **Inverse Proportion** — As one value increases, the other decreases at the same rate.

### Formulas You Must Know First
* **Work = Rate $\times$ Time** — The fundamental relationship for all work-based problems.

### Terms Used In This Pattern
* **Compositor** — A person who sets type for printing (treated as "Men").
* **Manuscript** — The document being worked on (treated as "Work").
