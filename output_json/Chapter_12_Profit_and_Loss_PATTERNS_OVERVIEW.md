# Profit And Loss

## What This Chapter Is About

This chapter deals with the financial mechanics of buying and selling goods, focusing on the relationship between the initial investment and the final revenue. Every problem revolves around the Cost Price (CP) and the Selling Price (SP), and how their difference determines the profitability of a transaction.

---

## Key Terms

*   **Cost Price (CP)** — The total amount spent to acquire an item, including any overheads or additional expenses.
*   **Selling Price (SP)** — The final amount received from a customer after selling an item.
*   **Profit (Gain)** — The surplus earned when `SP > CP`, calculated as `Profit = SP - CP`.
*   **Loss** — The deficit incurred when `SP < CP`, calculated as `Loss = CP - SP`.
*   **Marked Price (MP)** — The price labeled on an item, often used as the base for calculating discounts before reaching the final SP.

---

## Core Formulas

### [Profit/Loss Percentage]

$$
\text{Profit \%} = \left( \frac{\text{Profit}}{\text{CP}} \right) \times 100, \quad \text{Loss \%} = \left( \frac{\text{Loss}}{\text{CP}} \right) \times 100
$$

*When to use:* To express the gain or loss relative to the original investment.
*Variables:* `CP` = Cost Price; `Profit/Loss` = Absolute difference between SP and CP.

### [SP from CP]

$$
\text{SP} = \text{CP} \times \left( \frac{100 \pm \text{Profit/Loss \%}}{100} \right)
$$

*When to use:* To calculate the final selling price when the cost and percentage change are known.
*Variables:* `+` for Profit, `-` for Loss.

### [CP from SP]

$$
\text{CP} = \text{SP} \times \left( \frac{100}{100 \pm \text{Profit/Loss \%}} \right)
$$

*When to use:* To reverse-engineer the original cost when the selling price and percentage change are known.
*Variables:* `+` for Profit, `-` for Loss.

## How Patterns Connect

*   **Basic Profit/Loss** — distinct from the others because it involves simple, single-step calculations of gain or loss percentages.
*   **Bulk Purchase Free Items** — distinct from the others because the CP is spread over a quantity greater than the quantity paid for.
*   **CP = SP of Other Item** — distinct from the others because it links the cost of one object directly to the revenue of another.
*   **Chain Transactions** — distinct from the others because the SP of one transaction becomes the CP for the next in a sequence.
*   **Combined Transactions** — distinct from the others because it requires aggregating multiple items or stages into a single net profit/loss result.
*   **Cost Structure Changes** — distinct from the others because the CP itself is modified by a percentage before the final SP is determined.
*   **Data Interpretation: P&L** — distinct from the others because it requires extracting values from tables or graphs rather than word problems.
*   **Depreciation/VAT** — distinct from the others because it applies percentage changes to value over time or adds tax to the base price.
*   **Dishonest Dealer** — distinct from the others because the profit is generated by manipulating the weight or quantity rather than the price.
*   **Equivalent CP/SP** — distinct from the others because it focuses on finding a common base price for different items.
*   **Find CP from SP** — distinct from the others because it is a direct algebraic reversal of the standard SP formula.
*   **Find SP from CP** — distinct from the others because it is a direct application of the standard percentage markup/markdown.
*   **Fixed & Variable Costs** — distinct from the others because it splits the CP into static and fluctuating components.
*   **MP, Discount, Profit/Loss** — distinct from the others because it introduces a third price point (Marked Price) between CP and SP.
*   **Mixture Problems** — distinct from the others because it involves blending two items of different prices to achieve a target mean price.
*   **Price & Profit Scenarios** — distinct from the others because it compares two different hypothetical SPs to find a target profit.
*   **Profit/Loss in Units** — distinct from the others because it calculates gain/loss based on the number of items rather than total currency.
*   **Rate-Based Transactions** — distinct from the others because it deals with prices per unit (e.g., per kg or per dozen) rather than total sums.
*   **Relative Price & Profit** — distinct from the others because it uses ratios or proportions to compare prices instead of absolute values.
*   **Same SP, Equal % Gain/Loss** — distinct from the others because it specifically addresses the "no-profit, no-loss" trap when percentages are identical.
*   **Successive Discounts** — distinct from the others because it applies multiple percentage reductions to the MP sequentially.
*   **System of Equations: P&L** — distinct from the others because it requires solving for two unknown variables using two separate transaction scenarios.
*   **Uncategorized** — distinct from the others because these problems do not fit into the standard algorithmic categories above.

## Common Traps (Chapter-Wide)

*   **Base Confusion:** Always calculate profit or loss percentage on the **Cost Price (CP)**, never on the Selling Price (SP), unless explicitly stated.
*   **Percentage vs. Absolute Value:** Confusing a percentage change with an absolute currency value; always ensure you are applying the percentage to the correct base value.
