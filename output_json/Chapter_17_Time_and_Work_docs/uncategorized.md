# Time and Work: The "T-Days" Pattern

## Overview
This pattern involves two individuals (A and B) who complete a task together in $T$ days, where the individual times taken by A and B are expressed as offsets from that unknown $T$ (e.g., $T+x$ and $T+y$). The central idea is to equate the sum of their individual work rates to their combined work rate.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "Together complete in T days," "A alone in T + [number] days," "B alone in T + [number] days."
* **Given:** The time difference (extra days) taken by each person compared to the combined time.
* **Asked:** The value of $T$ (the time taken when working together).

---

## Key Formulas

### [The T-Days Shortcut]
$$
T = \sqrt{x \cdot y}
$$

**Variables:**
- $T$ = time taken by A and B together
- $x$ = extra days taken by A over $T$
- $y$ = extra days taken by B over $T$

**When to use:** When the question provides the "extra days" taken by each person individually relative to their combined time.

**Worked example:** If A takes $T+3$ days and B takes $T+12$ days, then $x=3$ and $y=12$.
$T = \sqrt{3 \times 12} = \sqrt{36} = 6$ days.

---

## Solution Framework

**Step 1: Define rates** — Write A's rate as $\frac{1}{T+x}$ and B's rate as $\frac{1}{T+y}$.
**Step 2: Set up equation** — Equate the sum of individual rates to the combined rate: $\frac{1}{T+x} + \frac{1}{T+y} = \frac{1}{T}$.
**Step 3: Simplify** — Cross-multiply to form the quadratic equation $T^2 = xy$.
**Step 4: Solve for T** — Take the square root of the product of the extra days.

---

## Shortcut Tricks

* **Trick:** $T = \sqrt{x \cdot y}$
* **Why it works:** The algebraic expansion of $\frac{1}{T+x} + \frac{1}{T+y} = \frac{1}{T}$ always simplifies to $T^2 = xy$ after canceling common terms.
* **When to use it:** Whenever the question follows the "T+x" and "T+y" format.
* **Example:** For $T+3$ and $T+12$, simply calculate $\sqrt{3 \times 12} = 6$. This skips 4 lines of algebraic manipulation.

---

## Common Mistakes

* **Mistake:** Adding the extra days ($x+y$) instead of multiplying them.
* **Why it happens:** Confusing the "sum of rates" logic with the "product of offsets" shortcut.
* **Fix:** Always remember the shortcut is the *square root of the product*.
* **Mistake:** Assuming $T$ must be one of the multiple-choice options.
* **Why it happens:** Panic during exams when the calculated value isn't listed.
* **Fix:** Trust the math; if $T=6$ and it's not there, select "None of these."

---

## Worked Example (Step-by-Step)

**Question:** A and B together complete a piece of work in T days. If A alone completes the work in T + 3 days and B alone completes the piece of work in T + 12 days, what is T?

**Solution:**
1. **Identify offsets:** Here, $x = 3$ and $y = 12$.
2. **Apply Shortcut:** Use the formula $T = \sqrt{x \cdot y}$.
3. **Calculate:** $T = \sqrt{3 \times 12} = \sqrt{36}$.
4. **Result:** $T = 6$.

**Answer:** 6 days.

---

## Similar Patterns

**Standard Time and Work:** If the question gives specific numbers (e.g., "A takes 10 days, B takes 15 days"), use the LCM method instead of the $T = \sqrt{xy}$ formula.

---

## Revision Summary

* **Key formula:** $T = \sqrt{x \cdot y}$
* **Spot it by:** Looking for "T+x" and "T+y" in the problem statement.
* **First move:** Identify the two extra day values ($x$ and $y$).
* **Fastest method:** Multiply the two extra values and take the square root.
* **Biggest trap:** Adding the extra days instead of multiplying them.

## Appendix: Prerequisites

### Concepts You Must Know
* **Work Rate** — The fraction of work completed in one unit of time (e.g., 1 day). If a job takes $n$ days, the rate is $\frac{1}{n}$.
* **Quadratic Equations** — Basic understanding of solving $T^2 = k$ by taking the square root.

### Formulas You Must Know First
$$
\text{Rate} = \frac{1}{\text{Time}}
$$
**What it means:** The speed of work is the reciprocal of the time taken.
**Example:** If a task takes 5 days, the daily work rate is $\frac{1}{5}$.

### Terms Used In This Pattern
* **T** — The unknown variable representing the total days taken by two people working together.
* **Piece of work** — The total task, usually represented as 1 (or 100%).
