# Stocks And Shares — Chapter Overview

## What This Chapter Is About

This chapter deals with the financial mechanics of corporate investment, specifically how companies raise capital by issuing shares to the public. The central relationship revolves around the distinction between the fixed "Face Value" of a share and its fluctuating "Market Value," and how these values determine the annual income (dividend) a shareholder receives. Problems in this chapter require you to calculate costs, compare the profitability of different investment options, and manage the impact of brokerage fees on your total returns.

---

## Core Concepts

**[The Nature of Capital]** — A company’s total capital is divided into small, equal units called shares. If a company needs ₹1,00,000 and divides it into 1,000 units, each share represents a ₹100 stake in the company.

**[Face Value vs. Market Value]** — Face Value (Nominal Value) is the permanent, printed value of a share, while Market Value is the price at which the share actually trades on the stock exchange. For example, a share with a Face Value of ₹100 might be trading at a Market Value of ₹120 because the company is performing well.

**[Dividend Mechanics]** — A dividend is the portion of company profit paid to shareholders, and it is **always** calculated as a percentage of the Face Value, never the Market Value. If a company declares a 10% dividend on a ₹100 share, you receive ₹10, regardless of whether you bought the share for ₹80 or ₹120.

**[Brokerage Impact]** — When buying or selling shares, you must pay a commission to a broker. This fee increases your total cost when buying (you pay more than the market price) and decreases your net cash when selling (you receive less than the market price).

---

## Key Terms Glossary

**Face Value (N.V.)** — The original value of a share as stated on the share certificate. It remains constant throughout the life of the share.

**Market Value (M.V.)** — The current price of a share in the open market. This is the price you actually pay to buy or receive when you sell.

**Dividend** — The annual profit distributed to shareholders, expressed as a percentage of the Face Value.

**Premium** — A state where the Market Value is higher than the Face Value (e.g., a ₹100 share selling for ₹110).

**Discount** — A state where the Market Value is lower than the Face Value (e.g., a ₹100 share selling for ₹90).

**Brokerage** — The commission charged by a broker for facilitating the trade, usually calculated as a percentage of the Market Value.

## Pattern Map

**[Stock Valuation]** (7 questions) — Focuses on determining the total cost or total cash realized based on the number of shares and their market price, including brokerage.

**[Income Calculation]** (12 questions) — Focuses on calculating the annual return (dividend) based on the total Face Value of shares held.

**[Investment Comparison]** (13 questions) — Focuses on determining which of two different stocks provides a better percentage return on the actual money invested.

**[Mixed Investment]** (7 questions) — Focuses on scenarios where an investor splits their capital across multiple stocks or adjusts their portfolio to achieve a specific total income.

**[Stock Reinvestment]** (3 questions) — Focuses on selling existing shares and using the proceeds to buy new shares, often to change the total annual income.

## Core Formulas

### Cost of Purchase
$$
\text{Total Cost} = (\text{Number of Shares} \times \text{Market Value}) + \text{Brokerage}
$$
**Variables:**
- $\text{Market Value}$ = Price per share in the market.
- $\text{Brokerage}$ = Fee per share (or total fee).

**When to use:** When calculating the total cash outflow required to acquire a specific quantity of stock.

**Key insight:** Always add brokerage to the market price before multiplying by the number of shares.

**Worked example:** Buying 100 shares at ₹100 with ₹1 brokerage: $100 \times (100 + 1) = ₹10,100$.

### Annual Income
$$
\text{Annual Income} = \text{Number of Shares} \times (\text{Dividend Rate} \times \text{Face Value})
$$
**Variables:**
- $\text{Dividend Rate}$ = Percentage of profit paid out.
- $\text{Face Value}$ = The nominal value of one share.

**When to use:** When calculating the total yearly return from a portfolio.

**Key insight:** Dividend is always calculated on Face Value, never on the Market Value or the amount invested.

**Worked example:** Holding 50 shares of ₹100 Face Value at 8% dividend: $50 \times (0.08 \times 100) = ₹400$.

### Rate of Return (Yield)
$$
\text{Rate of Return} = \left( \frac{\text{Annual Income}}{\text{Total Investment}} \right) \times 100
$$
**Variables:**
- $\text{Annual Income}$ = Total dividend received.
- $\text{Total Investment}$ = Total cash spent to buy the shares.

**When to use:** When comparing the efficiency of two different stock investments.

**Key insight:** The "better" investment is the one that yields a higher percentage return on the actual money spent.

**Worked example:** Investing ₹1000 to get ₹80 income: $(80 / 1000) \times 100 = 8\%$.

## Chapter-Wide Traps

**Confusing Face Value with Market Value:** Students often calculate dividends using the Market Value → Always use Face Value for dividends and Market Value for purchase/sale costs.

**Ignoring Brokerage:** Students often forget to add/subtract brokerage from the Market Value → Always check if the problem mentions brokerage, as it changes the effective price per share.

**Miscalculating "Number of Shares":** Students try to calculate shares based on the total investment without accounting for the Market Value → Always use $\frac{\text{Total Investment}}{\text{Market Value per share}}$ to find the quantity.
