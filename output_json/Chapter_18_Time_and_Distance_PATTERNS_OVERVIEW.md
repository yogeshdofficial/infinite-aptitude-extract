# Time And Distance — Chapter Overview

## What This Chapter Is About

This chapter deals with the mathematical relationship between the space covered (distance), the rate of movement (speed), and the duration of the journey (time). The central principle is the constant interplay between these three variables, where changing one inevitably affects the others. You will encounter problems ranging from simple unit conversions and basic motion to complex scenarios involving multiple modes of transport, relative movement between two objects, and interruptions during a journey.

---

## Core Concepts

**[The Fundamental Relationship]** — Speed is simply the rate at which distance is covered over time. If you know any two of these three variables, you can always calculate the third by rearranging the basic equation. For example, if you drive 100 km in 2 hours, your speed is 50 km/hr.

**[Unit Consistency]** — Calculations are only valid when all units match. You cannot multiply hours by meters per second; you must first convert all time units to seconds or hours, and all distance units to meters or kilometers. For example, if a speed is given in km/hr but the distance is in meters, you must convert the speed to m/sec before proceeding.

**[Inverse Proportionality of Time and Speed]** — When the distance remains constant, speed and time are inversely related. If you double your speed, you will cover the same distance in half the time. For example, if two people travel the same distance, the one with the higher speed will always take less time.

**[Relative Speed]** — This concept describes the speed of one object as observed from another moving object. When two objects move toward each other, their speeds add up because they are closing the gap faster; when they move in the same direction, their speeds subtract because the faster object is only slowly gaining on the slower one.

**[Average Speed]** — Average speed is not the simple arithmetic mean of two speeds. It is defined as the total distance covered divided by the total time taken. If you travel 100 km at 50 km/hr and 100 km at 20 km/hr, your average speed is not 35 km/hr, but rather the total 200 km divided by the total 7 hours spent.

---

## Key Terms Glossary

**Speed** — The distance traveled per unit of time, usually expressed in km/hr or m/sec.

**Relative Speed** — The effective speed between two moving objects. It is the sum of their speeds if they move in opposite directions and the difference of their speeds if they move in the same direction.

**Average Speed** — The total distance traveled divided by the total time taken for the entire journey, including any stops or variations in pace.

**Leap** — A unit of distance used in specific problems (often involving animals), representing the distance covered in a single jump.

## Core Formulas

### Basic Motion Formula
$$
\text{Distance} = \text{Speed} \times \text{Time}
$$
**Variables:** $D$ = Distance, $S$ = Speed, $T$ = Time.
**When to use:** Any scenario involving constant speed over a specific duration.
**Key insight:** Always ensure units (km vs m, hr vs sec) are aligned before calculating.
**Worked example:** A car at 60 km/hr for 2 hours covers $60 \times 2 = 120$ km.

### Unit Conversion (km/hr to m/sec)
$$
\text{Speed (m/sec)} = \text{Speed (km/hr)} \times \frac{5}{18}
$$
**Variables:** $S_{m/s}$ = Speed in m/sec, $S_{km/h}$ = Speed in km/hr.
**When to use:** When the speed is given in km/hr but the distance/time is in meters/seconds.
**Key insight:** The factor $\frac{5}{18}$ comes from $\frac{1000 \text{ meters}}{3600 \text{ seconds}}$.
**Worked example:** 36 km/hr becomes $36 \times \frac{5}{18} = 10$ m/sec.

### Average Speed (Equal Distances)
$$
\text{Average Speed} = \frac{2xy}{x+y}
$$
**Variables:** $x$ = speed for the first half, $y$ = speed for the second half.
**When to use:** Only when the distance covered at speed $x$ is exactly equal to the distance covered at speed $y$.
**Key insight:** This formula fails if the distances covered at the two speeds are different.
**Worked example:** Going to work at 30 km/hr and returning at 20 km/hr: $\frac{2 \times 30 \times 20}{30 + 20} = 24$ km/hr.

## Chapter-Wide Traps

**Unit Mismatch:** Using km/hr and meters in the same equation without conversion → Always convert everything to a single system (usually m/sec) before starting.

**Average Speed Fallacy:** Calculating the average of two speeds by simply adding and dividing by two → Always use the total distance divided by total time method.

**Ignoring Direction:** Forgetting to add or subtract speeds in relative speed problems → Always draw a quick sketch to see if objects are moving toward or away from each other.
