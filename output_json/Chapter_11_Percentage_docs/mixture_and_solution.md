# Mixture and Solution

## Overview
These questions involve changing the concentration of a mixture by adding, removing, or evaporating one component. The central idea is to **identify the constant component** (the part that does not change) and equate its quantity before and after the change.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "added," "evaporated," "fresh/dry fruit," "solution," "concentration," "water content."
* **Given:** Initial total weight, initial percentage of a component, and the final percentage of that same component.
* **Asked:** The amount of substance added/removed or the final total weight.
* **Scan for:** A component that remains constant (e.g., salt when water is added, pulp when water evaporates).

---

## Key Formulas

### [Constant Component Principle]
$$
\text{Weight}_{\text{initial}} \times \text{Percentage}_{\text{initial}} = \text{Weight}_{\text{final}} \times \text{Percentage}_{\text{final}}
$$

**Variables:**
- $\text{Weight}_{\text{initial/final}}$ = Total weight of the mixture before and after the change.
- $\text{Percentage}_{\text{initial/final}}$ = The percentage of the **constant** component in the mixture.

**When to use:** When one component (pulp, salt) remains unchanged while the other (water) is added or removed.

**Worked example:** Watermelon (20 kg) is 96% water (4% pulp). After evaporation, it is 95% water (5% pulp).
$20 \times 4\% = x \times 5\% \Rightarrow 80 = 5x \Rightarrow x = 16 \text{ kg}$.

---

## Solution Framework

**Step 1: Identify the constant component.** Determine which substance is not being added or evaporated (e.g., pulp in fruit, salt in a salt-water solution).
**Step 2: Calculate the constant amount.** Multiply the initial total weight by the percentage of the constant component.
**Step 3: Set up the equation.** Equate the constant amount to the new percentage of the final total weight.
**Step 4: Solve for the unknown.** Use basic algebra to find the missing weight or quantity.

---

## Shortcut Tricks
* **Trick:** Use the ratio of the constant component's percentages to find the ratio of total weights.
* **Why it works:** Since $W_1 \times P_1 = W_2 \times P_2$, then $\frac{W_1}{W_2} = \frac{P_2}{P_1}$.
* **When to use:** When you need to find the new weight quickly without writing out the full equation.
* **Example:** In Q326, pulp ratio is $4\% : 5\%$. The weight ratio is the inverse: $5 : 4$. Since $5 \text{ units} = 20 \text{ kg}$, then $1 \text{ unit} = 4 \text{ kg}$, so $4 \text{ units} = 16 \text{ kg}$.

---

## Common Mistakes
* **Mistake:** Using the water percentage instead of the pulp/salt percentage.
    * **Why it happens:** The question often gives the water percentage, and students use it automatically.
    * **Fix:** Always calculate the "constant" percentage first ($100\% - \text{water}\% = \text{constant}\%$).
* **Mistake:** Forgetting to add the added substance to the total weight in the denominator.
    * **Why it happens:** Treating the denominator as a constant.
    * **Fix:** If you add $x$ kg, the new total is $(\text{Initial Weight} + x)$.

---

## Worked Example (Step-by-Step)

**Question:** How many kg of pure salt must be added to 30 kg of 2% solution of salt and water to increase it to a 10% solution?

**Solution:**
1. **Identify constant:** Salt is being added, so **water** is the constant component.
2. **Calculate constant:** Initial water = $98\%$ of $30 = 29.4 \text{ kg}$.
3. **Set up equation:** Let $x$ be salt added. New total weight = $(30+x)$. New water percentage = $90\%$.
   $29.4 = 90\% \text{ of } (30+x) \Rightarrow 29.4 = 0.9(30+x)$.
4. **Solve:** $29.4 = 27 + 0.9x \Rightarrow 2.4 = 0.9x \Rightarrow x = \frac{24}{9} = 2\frac{2}{3} \text{ kg}$.

**Answer:** $2\frac{2}{3} \text{ kg}$.

---

## Similar Patterns
**Alligation:** Use this when you are mixing two different solutions of *different concentrations* to get a third concentration, rather than adding a pure substance to one solution.

---

## Revision Summary
* **Key formula:** $\text{Weight}_1 \times \%_1 = \text{Weight}_2 \times \%_2$.
* **Spot it by:** "Added," "evaporated," or "fresh/dry" keywords.
* **First move:** Find the percentage of the component that **doesn't** change.
* **Fastest method:** Use the inverse ratio of the constant component's percentages.
* **Biggest trap:** Using the water percentage instead of the solid/salt percentage.

---

## Appendix: Prerequisites

### Concepts You Must Know
* **Percentage:** A fraction with a denominator of 100. $x\% = \frac{x}{100}$.
* **Algebraic Equations:** Solving $ax + b = c$ for $x$.

### Formulas You Must Know First
* **Part-Whole Relationship:** $\text{Part} = \text{Total} \times \text{Percentage}$.

### Terms Used In This Pattern
* **Solute:** The substance dissolved in a solution (e.g., salt).
* **Solvent:** The liquid in which the solute is dissolved (e.g., water).
* **Concentration:** The amount of solute per unit of total solution.
