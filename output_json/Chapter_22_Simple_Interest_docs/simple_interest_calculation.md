# Simple Interest Calculation

## Overview
This pattern involves calculating the interest earned on a principal sum over a specific period at a fixed annual rate. The central idea is to ensure the time ($T$) is expressed in years and the rate ($R$) is in a standard percentage format before applying the formula.

---

## Recognition Clues
* **Keywords:** "Simple interest," "per annum," "amount," "borrowed," "lent out."
* **Given:** Principal ($P$), Rate ($R$), and Time ($T$) in days, months, or years.
* **Asked:** Simple Interest ($S.I.$) or Total Amount ($A = P + S.I.$).

---

## Key Formulas

### Simple Interest
$$
\text{S.I.} = \frac{P \times R \times T}{100}
$$

**Variables:**
- $P$ = Principal (the initial sum)
- $R$ = Rate of interest (percentage per annum)
- $T$ = Time (in years)

**When to use:** To find the interest earned on a sum over a specific duration.

**Worked example:** $P = 5760, R = 6\%, T = 3$ years.
$\text{S.I.} = \frac{5760 \times 6 \times 3}{100} = 1036.80$.

---

## Solution Framework

1. **Standardize $R$:** Convert mixed fractions (e.g., $6\frac{1}{4}\%$) into improper fractions (e.g., $\frac{25}{4}\%$) to avoid calculation errors.
2. **Standardize $T$:** If time is in months, divide by 12; if in days, divide by 365 (unless it's a leap year).
3. **Apply Formula:** Substitute $P, R,$ and $T$ into $\frac{P \times R \times T}{100}$.
4. **Calculate Amount (if asked):** Add the calculated $S.I.$ to the original $P$ to get the final amount.

---

## Shortcut Tricks

* **Trick:** Use the "Fractional Rate" method.
* **Why it works:** Multiplying by a fraction is often faster than converting to decimals and dividing by 100.
* **When to use:** When $R$ is a complex fraction like $16\frac{2}{3}\%$ or $6\frac{1}{4}\%$.
* **Example:** For $R = 16\frac{2}{3}\% = \frac{50}{3}\%$, use $\frac{1}{6}$ as the multiplier if the time is 1 year.

---

## Common Mistakes

* **Mistake:** Using months or days directly as $T$ without converting to years.
    * **Fix:** Always divide months by 12 and days by 365.
* **Mistake:** Miscounting days in a date range (e.g., Feb 4th to April 18th).
    * **Fix:** Exclude the start date, include the end date.
* **Mistake:** Confusing Simple Interest with Compound Interest.
    * **Fix:** Check if the question explicitly says "Simple Interest."

---

## Worked Example (Step-by-Step)

**Question:** Find the simple interest on ₹ 3000 at $6\frac{1}{4}\%$ per annum from 4th Feb 2009 to 18th April 2009.

**Solution:**
1. **Standardize $R$:** $6\frac{1}{4}\% = \frac{25}{4}\%$.
2. **Standardize $T$:** 
   - Feb: $28 - 4 = 24$ days.
   - March: 31 days.
   - April: 18 days.
   - Total = $24 + 31 + 18 = 73$ days.
   - $T = \frac{73}{365} = \frac{1}{5}$ year.
3. **Apply Formula:** $S.I. = \frac{3000 \times \frac{25}{4} \times \frac{1}{5}}{100} = \frac{3000 \times 25}{100 \times 4 \times 5} = \frac{3000 \times 25}{2000} = 37.50$.

**Answer:** ₹ 37.50.

---

## Similar Patterns
**Compound Interest:** Distinguished by the phrase "compounded annually/half-yearly" instead of "simple interest."

---

## Revision Summary
* **Key formula:** $S.I. = \frac{P \times R \times T}{100}$.
* **Spot it by:** "Simple interest" and "per annum" keywords.
* **First move:** Convert $T$ to years and $R$ to an improper fraction.
* **Fastest method:** Simplify fractions before multiplying.
* **Biggest trap:** Forgetting to convert months/days to years.

---

## Appendix: Prerequisites

### Concepts You Must Know
* **Fractions:** Converting mixed numbers to improper fractions (e.g., $a\frac{b}{c} = \frac{ac+b}{c}$).
* **Time Conversion:** $1 \text{ year} = 12 \text{ months} = 365 \text{ days}$.

### Formulas You Must Know First
* **Amount:** $A = P + S.I.$ (Total money returned).

### Terms Used In This Pattern
* **Principal ($P$):** The original sum of money borrowed or lent.
* **Rate ($R$):** The interest percentage charged per year.
* **Time ($T$):** The duration for which the money is borrowed.
