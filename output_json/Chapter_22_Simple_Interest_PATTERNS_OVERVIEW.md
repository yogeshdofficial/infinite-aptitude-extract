# Simple Interest — Chapter Overview

## What This Chapter Is About

Simple Interest deals with the cost of borrowing or lending money where the interest is calculated exclusively on the original amount borrowed, regardless of how much time has passed. The central relationship is a linear growth model where the interest earned is directly proportional to the principal, the rate, and the time. Problems in this chapter range from basic calculations of interest and principal to more complex scenarios involving changing interest rates, split investments, and repayment through instalments.

---

## Core Concepts

**[The Principal]** — This is the "base" amount of money that is either borrowed from a lender or deposited into an account. It is the starting point for all calculations, and in Simple Interest, the interest is always calculated based on this original sum, never on the interest that has already accumulated. *Example: If you deposit ₹10,000 into a savings account, that ₹10,000 is your Principal.*

**[The Rate of Interest]** — This represents the "price" of using money, usually expressed as a percentage per year (per annum). It tells you what fraction of the principal must be paid as interest for every year the money is borrowed. *Example: A rate of 5% p.a. means you pay ₹5 in interest for every ₹100 borrowed for one year.*

**[Time Period]** — This is the duration for which the money is borrowed or invested. In Simple Interest, time must always be expressed in years to match the annual rate; if a problem gives time in months or days, it must be converted into a fraction of a year. *Example: 6 months is treated as $\frac{6}{12}$ or $0.5$ years.*

**[The Amount]** — This is the total sum returned to the lender at the end of the term, which consists of the original Principal plus the total Simple Interest earned. *Example: If you borrow ₹1,000 and pay ₹150 in interest, the total Amount you pay back is ₹1,150.*

---

## Key Terms Glossary

**Principal ($P$)** — The original sum of money lent or borrowed.

**Simple Interest ($S.I.$)** — The extra money paid for the use of the principal, calculated as a fixed percentage of the principal per unit of time.

**Rate ($R$)** — The annual percentage at which interest is charged on the principal.

**Time ($T$)** — The duration of the loan or investment, measured in years.

**Amount ($A$)** — The final total value, calculated as $A = P + S.I.$

## Pattern Map

1. **Simple Interest Calculation** (13 questions) — The foundational pattern where you are given three variables ($P, R, T$) and asked to find the fourth ($S.I.$).
2. **Find Missing Variable** (44 questions) — You are given the interest and two other variables, requiring you to rearrange the formula to solve for the unknown ($P, R,$ or $T$).
3. **Comparative Interest** (12 questions) — Involves comparing two different scenarios to see how changing one variable affects the interest or the amount.
4. **Rate Change Impact** (7 questions) — Focuses on how a shift in the interest rate percentage alters the total interest earned or the final amount.
5. **Multi-Phase Interest** (4 questions) — Deals with scenarios where the interest rate changes over different time intervals (e.g., different rates for the first 2 years vs. the next 3 years).
6. **Split Investment** (31 questions) — The principal is divided into two or more parts, each invested at different rates or for different times, requiring you to solve a system of equations.
7. **Instalment Debt** (8 questions) — Focuses on paying off a debt through periodic payments, where the interest is calculated on the remaining balance.
8. **Uncategorized** (6 questions) — Miscellaneous problems that require applying the core formula in non-standard contexts.

## Core Formulas

### Simple Interest Calculation
$$
S.I. = \frac{P \times R \times T}{100}
$$
**Variables:** $P$ = Principal, $R$ = Rate per annum, $T$ = Time in years.
**When to use:** When you need to find the interest earned on a sum over a specific period.
**Key insight:** Always ensure $T$ is in years; if given in days, divide by 365.
**Worked example:** $P=1000, R=5\%, T=2 \implies S.I. = \frac{1000 \times 5 \times 2}{100} = 100$.

### Finding Principal
$$
P = \frac{S.I. \times 100}{R \times T}
$$
**Variables:** $S.I.$ = Simple Interest, $R$ = Rate, $T$ = Time.
**When to use:** When the interest earned is known, and you need to find the original sum.
**Key insight:** This is just a rearrangement of the base formula; don't memorize it as a new concept.
**Worked example:** $S.I.=200, R=5\%, T=2 \implies P = \frac{200 \times 100}{5 \times 2} = 2000$.

2. **Find Missing Variable** — Move here to practice algebraic manipulation of the core formula.
3. **Comparative Interest & Rate Change Impact** — These build on your ability to handle variables by comparing two scenarios.
4. **Multi-Phase Interest & Split Investment** — These are more advanced applications requiring you to set up multiple equations.
5. **Instalment Debt** — Study last as it requires a conceptual shift in how the principal is reduced over time.

## Chapter-Wide Traps

**Time Unit Mismatch:** Students often forget to convert months or days into years → Always divide days by 365 and months by 12 before plugging into the formula.

**Confusing Interest with Amount:** Questions often ask for the "Amount" but students stop after calculating the "Interest" → Always re-read the final question to see if you need to add the Principal back to the Interest.

**Ignoring the "Per Annum" Clause:** Assuming the rate applies to the whole period regardless of time → Always multiply the rate by the time in years to get the effective interest percentage.
